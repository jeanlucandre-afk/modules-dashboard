# Overused AI Patterns Reference

This document catalogs AI-generated writing patterns that must be avoided to create human-sounding text.

## Prohibited Rhetorical Constructions

### 1. Elliptical Setup (Implied Question Pattern)
Uses a fragmentary rhetorical question to create intrigue and rhythm.

**Examples to avoid:**
- "The best part? You don't lift a finger."
- "The only limitation? Claude can be too cautious for bold creative work."
- "The f*cked part? They didn't pay a dime."
- "The wild thing? It actually worked."
- "The scary truth? You're already competing with it."

**Replacement strategy:** State the point directly without the setup question.

### 2. Revelation Hook (Contrarian Reveal)
Positions the speaker as exposing a hidden truth or unspoken reality.

**Examples to avoid:**
- "Here's the truth no one is talking about..."
- "What nobody tells you about AI..."
- "The thing nobody admits in marketing..."
- "No one's saying this out loud, but..."
- "Here's what they won't tell you about success..."
- "Everyone loves to talk about growth, but no one talks about the cost."

**Replacement strategy:** Present the information directly or frame it as observation rather than revelation.

### 3. The Big Contrast (Antithesis)
Takes an obvious assumption and flips it with a grand insight.

**Examples to avoid:**
- "It's not automation—it's amplification."
- "It's not about more data—it's about better decisions."
- "You're not buying software—you're buying time."
- "It's not speed—it's precision."
- "It's not attention—it's trust."
- "It's not scale—it's depth."

**Replacement strategies:**
- State the positive directly
- Reframe with emphasis on what matters
- Describe only what it IS, not what it ISN'T

### 4. The Great Reframe (Conceptual Reversal / Chiasmus)
States a universal truth, then reframes it with a more grounded or moral insight.

**Examples to avoid:**
- "Everyone's chasing attention—few are earning trust."
- "Speed doesn't win—momentum does."
- "The goal isn't to sound human—it's to be understood."
- "People want answers—but they remember stories."
- "We measure clicks—but we should measure conviction."
- "AI won't make you better—it'll make you more of what you already are."

**Replacement strategy:** Express a single coherent thought rather than a balanced opposition.

### 5. Philosophical Reduction (Paradox / Aphoristic Reduction)
Reduces a big idea to a simple, poetic contradiction or truth.

**Examples to avoid:**
- "AI isn't replacing people—it's replacing waiting."
- "Confidence isn't loud—it's quiet consistency."
- "Freedom isn't having more choices—it's knowing which ones matter."
- "Leadership isn't control—it's clarity."
- "Success isn't more—it's enough."
- "Creativity isn't invention—it's attention."

**Replacement strategy:** Describe the concept directly without paradoxical framing.

## Prohibited Constructions

### "It's not X, but Y" Construction
**Pattern to avoid:**
- "It's not X, but Y"
- "It isn't about X, it's about Y"
- "A Rolex is not just made—it is engineered with obsession"

**Replacement strategies:**
- State the positive directly
- Reframe with emphasis
- Describe only the "Y"
- Use contrast with "while" or "instead"

### Em-dash Usage
**Strict rule:** Avoid the em-dash (—) completely in all contexts.

**Replacement strategies:**
- Use periods to create separate sentences
- Use commas for parenthetical information
- Use colons for explanatory information
- Restructure sentences to eliminate the need for punctuation breaks
