# STS_04: Video Essay Script

> 10 minutes. Anchor: Morozov, *To Save Everything, Click Here* (2013). Read alongside: Andreessen (2023), Barbrook & Cameron (1995), Zuboff (2019). Voice: first person, JL — natural, conversational, working-through. No em-dashes in JL prose. Page-cited quotes shown on screen.

**Submission:** MP4 link.

## BEAT 1. Cold open (0:00 – 0:30)

There is one sentence in Morozov's book that I keep getting stuck on. It is on page fourteen of the introduction, before the actual book has even started, and it is about freedom needing the option of doing the wrong thing. The other thing I keep getting stuck on is the tone of the rest of the book, which is angrier than it needs to be, and I have come to think the anger is a tell. You do not get that worked up at a worldview unless its assumptions were once your own. So I want to spend the next ten minutes treating this book as a document of someone trying to write his way out of a position he is still standing partly inside, because that version is more honest than the one Morozov thinks he wrote.

## BEAT 2. What Morozov is actually doing (0:30 – 3:00)

Morozov defines solutionism on page five.

`[QUOTE CARD: "an unhealthy preoccupation with sexy, monumental, and narrow-minded solutions—the kind of stuff that wows audiences at TED Conferences—to problems that are extremely complex, fluid, and contentious." (Morozov, p. 5)]`

I kind of dismissed it the first time. The second time I started paying attention to the words he reaches for, and they are a bit strange. *Sexy. Monumental. Narrow-minded.* These are not design words. He is calling these things vulgar, not just bad. Tara Brabazon, reviewing the book in *Times Higher Education*, picked up on this and described the internet of Morozov's targets as "an untouchable deity, slippery, shiny and omnipotent." I think she is right that he is trying to deflate it as a kind of religion.

There is something else going on in his prose that I want to point at. Watch the verbs. Solutions get *deployed*. Problems get *recast*. Friction gets *eliminated*. There is no subject in any of those sentences. The passive voice quietly removes the person who decided the problem was a problem in the first place, and that is exactly the move Morozov is trying to criticise. He is criticising a worldview whose central trick is the disappearing actor, in sentences that perform the same disappearance. I do not think this is hypocrisy. I think it is more interesting than that. He is standing inside the language he is trying to break out of, and the strain is audible. If solutionism has a priesthood, and Brabazon's deity language pushes me to think it does, the priests are not the founders, because the founders are too obvious. The priests are the product managers, because they are the ones who actually write the sentence that turns a human situation into a ticket. The book never names them, which feels less like an oversight and more like a blind spot.

The deeper move he makes is that solutionists decide what counts as a problem before they ask whether it is one. BinCam, the smart bin that posts your rubbish to Facebook, does not solve a recycling problem. It redefines throwing rubbish away as something that needs an audience, and once you have defined it that way, of course you build a bin with a camera. The framing has already done the work. Once you see this move you cannot un-see it in product copy. The word Morozov reaches for is *solutionism*, and I think it is too small for what is going on. The closer word is *enclosure*, in the eighteenth-century sense where common land got fenced and rented back to the people who used to walk across it. The smart bin fences a kitchen. A streak fences a friendship. A civic check-in fences voting. The fences are the product.

The third move is internet-centrism, where I want to push on him a bit. Morozov treats it as a category mistake, like founders are just confused about what the internet is. I think it is worse than confusion. When a founder says *the internet wants this*, they are doing a small piece of political work, which is to relocate the source of a decision from a person who can be held responsible to a noun that cannot. Morozov diagnoses the noun. He does not chase the move, and the move is the part that has political consequences, because diffuse responsibility is the form most contemporary harm actually takes.

`[VISUAL: cut to examples.]`

His examples are deliberately small. The smart bin. The teapot that glows red when the grid is overloaded. Voting turned into a Foursquare check-in. Morozov reads each one as replacing a moral act with a tracked metric. I would put it slightly differently. Each one replaces a relationship with an interface. The bin used to involve you and the council. Now it involves you and a feed. The second party has been quietly removed, and the removal is the part that matters, because ethics needs two. You cannot have a duty to a mirror.

Then there is this sentence on page fourteen.

`[QUOTE CARD: "Imperfection, ambiguity, opacity, disorder, and the opportunity to err, to sin, to do the wrong thing: all of these are constitutive of human freedom." (Morozov, p. xiv)]`

This is the one I keep coming back to. People read it as Morozov defending bad behaviour, like he is saying we need the freedom to be lazy. I do not think that is the claim. I think the claim is structural. A chosen good and a defaulted good are not the same object, because the choosing was part of what made the good good in the first place. The strongest version of his argument, which he never quite says out loud, is that ethics is path-dependent. The wrong answers you arrived at on the way are part of what makes the right answer mean anything when you finally get there. Take the wrong ones away and you do not have ethics. You have compliance.

## BEAT 3. Andreessen, ten years later (3:00 – 4:15)

The book is from 2013, so a fair test is whether any of this still applies thirteen years later. In 2023, Marc Andreessen published *The Techno-Optimist Manifesto*.

`[QUOTE CARD: "We believe that there is no material problem—whether created by nature or by technology—that cannot be solved with more technology." (Andreessen, a16z.com, 16 Oct 2023)]`

I had to read this a few times to work out what kind of claim it is. The load-bearing word in that sentence is not *technology*, it is *material*. The sentence quietly excludes any non-material problem from the category of problems worth solving, which is a bigger move than the surface optimism. It is not "we can fix everything", it is "the things we cannot fix are not real problems". A definitional victory dressed up as a confidence statement, which is the same shape as the smart bin. Redefine the territory, then claim the redefinition as a result.

Two sociologists, Nachtwey and Seidl, writing in *Theory, Culture & Society*, made an argument I keep coming back to. They say the solutionist entrepreneur tends to believe that "if they are doing good, they will also do well, and conversely, that if they are doing well, they must also be doing good," which is older than Silicon Valley by quite a lot. That is basically Weber's Protestant ethic. I want to push on it though. Weber's protestants thought worldly success was *evidence* of grace. The founder version is stronger. It says the building of the company is itself the moral act, regardless of what the company does, which means the moral charge attaches to the form of the activity, not its content. That is closer to a sacrament than to Weber, and sacraments are unfalsifiable on purpose. Once I read the manifesto as something closer to a creed than to an argument, the strangeness of it kind of resolved. It is not trying to convince anyone. It is trying to constitute a community of people who already agree.

## BEAT 4. The three taglines, slowly (4:15 – 5:40)

I pulled three taglines from current AI tool homepages this week. *Creativity, solved. We removed the boring parts of work. Your team, but ten times faster.* I want to read each one with Morozov in mind.

Take the first one. *Creativity, solved.* The obvious objection is that creativity is not the kind of thing that gets solved. That is the cheap one. The interesting thing is the comma. The comma turns *creativity* into a noun on a shelf, an item that can carry a state, like *dishwasher, installed*. The grammar is doing the philosophical work before the verb arrives. Once you have made creativity comma-able, you have already conceded it is the kind of thing that can be in a state of completion, which is the entire claim the tagline is supposedly making. The argument has been won at the level of punctuation.

The second one is harder for me. *We removed the boring parts of work.* The person who wrote that probably has something specific in mind, like formatting documents, and honestly I would also like fewer of those. The first defence I reached for was cognitive, that boredom produces the kind of undirected attention which turns into actual thought. That defence is real but it lets the writer of the tagline off the hook, because it makes the disagreement about psychology. The deeper objection is about who decides what counts as boring. The sentence assumes the boring part and the meaningful part are separable, which is the same assumption Morozov keeps catching solutionists making. In most jobs I have watched up close, they are not separable. The boring bit and the bit where you actually understand what you are doing are often the same bit, viewed from two angles. I am not certain I have this right. I am certain the tagline is making a bet about boredom that the writer probably did not realise they were making.

The third one fails differently. *Your team, but ten times faster.* The reason a small team takes time on a hard decision is usually that the decision is hard. The bottleneck is not execution, it is figuring out what you actually believe. You cannot get to a hard decision faster by adding workers. You can only get to a confident-sounding decision faster, which is a different object, and most of the time a worse one. The tagline solves a problem most teams do not have, by accelerating the part of the work that was never the problem.

There is one more thing the three taglines do that Morozov does not name. They each speak to *you*, the buyer, while describing a collective act. *Your* team. *Your* creativity. The pronoun does political work. It hands the tool to one person and asks them to apply it to a group, which is structurally how a manager talks. The taglines are not consumer copy. They are management copy in consumer clothing, and that slippage is the sale. I work in this space, so I am not pretending to be neutral here.

## BEAT 5. Where Morozov stops, and what the other texts let me say (5:40 – 7:50)

The honest part of the video is the next two minutes, because Morozov is also wrong in places, and I would rather say so than not.

The first thing he gets wrong is how flat his categories become by the end of the book. There is a moment where he is putting an app that nudges you to drink water and a pretrial risk-assessment algorithm in the same box, both labelled solutionism. I do not think this is just a proportion problem. I think it is a category problem. The water app reframes a behaviour. The algorithm reframes a *judgement*, and judgements have due-process histories that behaviours do not. To call both of them solutionism implies they share a mechanism, and I do not think they do. One is a small commercial nudge. The other is the state laundering its decisions through software, and *laundering* is the right word, because the technical layer is absorbing the moral responsibility that used to attach to a human signature. Farhad Manjoo, debating Morozov in *Slate*, said Morozov's Silicon Valley "bears little resemblance" to the one he covers as a tech reporter. Manjoo was being slightly unfair, but the structural point sticks. Morozov needs a second word for the algorithm case, and not having one means his critique lands hardest on the targets that matter least.

The second thing is the *LibrarianShipwreck* point, that Morozov "offers a critique from the status quo, for the status quo." Sebastian Payne, in the *Spectator*, called the book "a thesis from an author in mid-thought," which actually really landed for me, but the *LibrarianShipwreck* line is sharper, because it names the structural problem. What I think it means concretely is this. Morozov writes as if the problem is bad ideas held by powerful people, and as if better ideas held by better people would produce better products. But the products keep arriving regardless of what anyone thinks of them, which suggests the ideas are not really upstream of the products. The ideas are something more like a receipt, issued after the transaction to make the transaction feel coherent. Morozov is treating ideology as cause. I think it is closer to a receipt.

This is where I needed the other two texts.

`[QUOTE CARD: "a bizarre fusion of the cultural bohemianism of San Francisco with the hi-tech industries of Silicon Valley." (And in a later sentence:) "in the digital utopia, everybody will be both hip and rich." (Barbrook & Cameron, 1995)]`

Barbrook and Cameron, writing eighteen years before Morozov, basically name the worldview he is angry at. They describe this fusion where you have got New Left individualism on top and neoliberal markets underneath, fused so tightly that "freeing the individual" and "deregulating capital" end up sounding like the same sentence. Reading them alongside Morozov, I started to see why he can describe the products but not stop them. They are downstream of an ideology much older than any of them, and the ideology rewards exactly that shape of product. Morozov is annoyed at the output. Barbrook and Cameron explain why the output is profitable, which is the part Morozov needs and does not have.

`[QUOTE CARD: "Surveillance capitalism unilaterally claims human experience as free raw material for translation into behavioral data." (Zuboff, 2019, p. 8)]`

Zuboff names what happens economically once a problem has been reframed. Once a thing has been recast as raw material, it has a price, and once it has a price you build a factory around it. I want to push this one step further than she does. Once experience is raw material, having an experience is itself a productive act, which means leisure is now labour, and the old line between work and not-work collapses from the not-work side. The smart bin is not just a recasting of rubbish. It is a small annexation of the kitchen into the workplace, and the worker is unpaid because the worker has been redescribed as a user.

Morozov on his own is the top of a stack and he explains the least. Barbrook and Cameron sit underneath him on ideology. Zuboff sits underneath them on economics. You need all three to say anything that holds up, and the order matters, because the bottom of the stack is what generates the top.

## BEAT 6. What I take from the book (7:50 – 9:15)

Three things, and one question I am keeping.

The first is that the book turned out to be more useful as a reading practice than as an argument. I do not really use Morozov's framework. I use his ear. He taught me to listen for the verb in a product sentence and ask what the verb is hiding, which is a smaller skill than a theory, and it is the thing of his I have actually kept. That probably tells you something about which parts of any critique survive and which parts evaporate.

The second is a sharper version of his test. Morozov asks whether a problem is complex, fluid, and contentious, and basically any honest problem passes. I have been trying a different question. If this tool disappeared tomorrow, would the people it serves notice the absence of the tool, or the absence of the problem? If they would notice the problem coming back, the tool was solving something. If they would only notice the tool's absence, the tool was the problem's source. I am pretty sure this fails for medical tools for reasons I have not worked out yet, but it is more useful to me than what Morozov gives, because it makes you specify the counterfactual rather than gesture at it.

The third is harder to say, and it is also where I am still kind of stuck. The book is, on close reading, a young person's book, and I mean that as a compliment. The contempt has the energy of someone who has not yet decided to make peace with the industry, and that energy is most of what the book has going for it. Barbrook and Cameron sound almost weary. Morozov does not. The roughness is the source of the usefulness, and any attempt to smooth it would be the same mistake as smoothing a protest sign.

`[VISUAL: pull quote.]`

The question I am keeping is the inversion of the obvious one. Everyone asks whether tools create the problems they solve. The harder question, which Morozov avoids, is whether *critiques* create the problems they name. If I listen for solutionism in every tagline, I am going to find solutionism in every tagline, which might mean the category is real, or might mean it is a frame I am imposing. I do not know how to tell from inside the frame, and I am suspicious of any critic who claims they can. Including Morozov. Including, on the bad days, me.

## BEAT 7. Close (9:15 – 10:00)

Morozov is half right on purpose, I think. The other half is happening inside the companies he is suspicious of, by people who have read him and decided to keep building anyway. I do not think he would be surprised. I think he would say it is exactly his point, and *LibrarianShipwreck* would say his point is also the limit of his politics.

Kranzberg basically settled the neutrality question in 1986, and we keep re-litigating it.

`[CARD: "Technology is neither good nor bad; nor is it neutral." (Kranzberg, *Technology and Culture* 27:3, 1986, p. 545)]`

The reason that sentence keeps working is that it refuses the binary the rest of the conversation insists on. A tool's moral charge gets decided by the conditions of its use, and the conditions are usually older than the tool and more powerful than its maker, which is also why founder intent is the least interesting variable in any honest analysis of a product.

The question I want to leave with is not whether your tool is neutral. Kranzberg already settled that. The harder question is which of your defaults you would actually be willing to defend in public, by name, on camera, the way I am trying to right now. And which problems you would have to admit you invented.

Fade.
