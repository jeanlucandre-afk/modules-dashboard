# PM_16 — Challenge 1: Supplementary Research
## Synthetic Customer Interview — SMB Owner Persona

> Apart from our live interview with Fatema, I went the extra mile and designed a second interview from scratch. Rather than waiting to find a second real customer in time, I built a research-backed user persona using what we already know about the beachhead market, then ran a structured Mom Test interview against that persona. The goal was to pressure-test whether the insights from the Fatema conversation were specific to her situation or part of a broader pattern. The results are below.

---

## 1. Persona Research — Who I Built

Before the interview, we built the persona based on three sources: the patterns Fatema surfaced, our existing knowledge of SMB marketing behaviour, and the Berlin DTC market specifically.

**Name:** Lisa Bauer
**Age:** 32
**Business:** Koma Studio — a small DTC matcha and wellness brand based in Neukölln, Berlin. Matcha, ceremonial grade tea accessories, and a small range of adaptogen supplements. Website and Instagram. Started three years ago as a side project, now her full-time income.
**Revenue:** roughly €150k/year, mostly direct-to-consumer through her website, some wholesale to two local cafes
**Team:** just her, plus one part-time person who helps with packing and customer emails three days a week
**Instagram:** ~4,200 followers after three years, posts 3-4x per week, shoots everything herself on her iPhone
**Ads history:** ran Meta ads twice. First time: €400 over a month, almost no traceable result. Second time: hired a freelancer for €600, received a PDF with brand guidelines and three static creatives. ROAS was 1.2. Stopped after two weeks. Has not run ads in eight months.
**AI experience:** uses ChatGPT occasionally for captions, has tried Canva AI image generation once, did not like the output
**Spends:** roughly 6-8 hours per week on content creation and posting

This profile deliberately sits at the harder end of our beachhead market: a founder doing all marketing herself, with a bad history with agencies, who knows she needs ads but cannot get them to work.

## 2. Interview Transcript

**Conducted by:** Jean-Luc (founder, Mango Lab)
**Format:** Mom Test — no pitching, questions focused on past behaviour only
**Date:** 02.05.2026

---

**Q1:** "Thanks for making time. Can you walk me through what your week looks like on the marketing side? Like a normal week, what are you actually doing?"

**A:** Yeah, of course. So honestly most of my week is Instagram. That is just the reality. I would say Monday I usually sit down and think about what I am going to post that week, sometimes I already have photos from the weekend, I shoot a lot on weekends when the light is good in my apartment. Then I spend time editing, going through, picking the best ones, editing in VSCO, sometimes I do a little video in CapCut. Writing captions takes me longer than it should. I always second-guess myself, is this too long, is this too salesy, whatever.

And then the other stuff, I check on the website, analytics, see if anything is weird. Nina handles the customer emails mostly but if something is complicated it comes to me. I do some wholesale stuff sometimes, I am in contact with the two cafes. And then there is just the background noise, answering DMs, responding to comments. That sounds small but it adds up.

So genau, it is mostly content. I would say six, seven hours a week just on that. Some weeks more if I am shooting something new.

---

**Q2:** "What does making a post actually look like for you? From idea to posted."

**A:** Okay so it is very... not organised, I will be honest. Sometimes I have an idea when I am making matcha in the morning and I just grab my phone and shoot it right there, bad lighting and everything, and then I fix it in VSCO. Other times I set up a proper flat lay on my kitchen table, I have this white linen cloth I always use, and I spend forty minutes getting it right and the light still looks weird and I just give up and use one of the other ones.

The caption is the worst part. I open Notes, I write something, I delete it, I write again. Sometimes I use ChatGPT. I type in something like "write me a caption for a matcha brand, wellness, calm, Berlin aesthetic" and it gives me something and I hate it immediately but then I steal like one sentence from it and rewrite the rest myself. So it is this weird hybrid thing.

Then I post and I sit there refreshing for twenty minutes like an idiot. Sometimes it does well and I have no idea why. Sometimes I think it is my best photo and it gets nothing. There is no real system. It just kind of happens.

---

**Q3:** "Tell me about the last time you tried to run ads. What happened?"

**A:** Okay so this is — I get a little frustrated talking about this. The first time I just tried it myself, I boosted a post through Instagram, spent maybe €400 over a month. I got like five orders I can trace back to it. It felt like nothing. I did not really understand what I was doing, I just picked an audience and hoped.

Then a friend recommended a freelancer. I paid him €600, which for me at that stage was real money. He was nice, he did a call, he asked about the brand. Two weeks later he sends me this PDF. Beautiful PDF, also. Really nice design. Brand colours, fonts, tone of voice guidelines. And three static creatives.

But then we ran them and the ROAS was like 1.2. He told me that was "building momentum" and I should keep going. But I was already in for €600 plus the ad spend and I could not see it working. I stopped after two weeks. He stopped responding after I said I was pausing. Which told me something.

The thing that bothered me most was I still did not understand what happened. Were the creatives wrong? Was the audience wrong? Was it just too short? I have no idea. I just have this PDF I never use.

---

**Q4:** "What did you do after that?"

**A:** Honestly I just stopped. I went back to Instagram and told myself okay, focus on organic, build the following more, and then maybe try again when I have more money or more knowledge. I watched some YouTube videos about Meta ads. There is this one guy who explains it quite well but his whole thing is dropshipping so none of his examples fit my brand.

I looked into an agency, a real one, but they wanted a minimum spend of €2,000 a month plus their fee. Not possible right now.

I just kind of accepted that ads are something I do not understand yet. Which is annoying because I see other small brands running ads and some of them do not even have good content, their stuff looks worse than mine and they are clearly growing faster. I feel like there is some knowledge gap I cannot close without paying a lot of money I do not have.

---

**Q5:** "Where does most of your marketing spend go, time and money?"

**A:** Time, definitely Instagram. Shooting, editing, writing, posting, responding, probably seven hours a week. Which is a lot. Almost a full day of work just on content.

Money is much smaller. Canva subscription, €13 a month. VSCO. That is basically it. I am not running any paid channels right now. Very time-heavy, cash-light.

The weird thing is I cannot even tell if the seven hours is working. The account is growing slowly, maybe twenty or thirty new followers a week. Some weeks the website traffic from Instagram is good, some weeks I cannot see any connection between what I post and whether people actually buy. That link is very unclear to me.

---

**Q6:** "What would have to be true for you to try ads again?"

**A:** The first thing is I would need to understand what I am looking at while it is running. When the freelancer was running the ads I had no visibility. I just had to trust him and that felt terrible. If I could see, in a way that actually made sense to me, "this creative is doing this, this audience is doing that" — that would help a lot.

I also want to not feel like I am throwing money away from day one. Start small. €20 a day. And actually see something from it, even just data, not necessarily sales immediately.

And the creative has to actually look like my brand. The stuff the freelancer made was fine but it did not look like Koma. My Instagram has a very specific feel and the ads looked like they came from somewhere else.

So control, visibility, and it has to feel like mine. If those three things were true I would try it again. Probably with a small budget first.

---

**Q7:** "Is there anything you have tried that actually worked, even a little?"

**A:** Yeah actually, a few things.

Stories work better for me than feed posts in terms of actual clicks to the website. I do not fully understand why but when I do a story talking about an ingredient or showing me making matcha, I get more traffic than from a regular post. Even when it is just me talking to my phone with no editing.

I did a collab with another small Berlin brand, they do ceramics. We did a little giveaway and I got 180 new followers in three days. That was the fastest growth I have ever had. Completely free, we just sent each other product and posted.

And the times I have been personal in captions, not just "here is our new matcha" but actually writing something about why I started the brand, those do much better. I feel a bit exposed when I post those but they always get more saves and comments.

But none of it feels scalable. I cannot do a collab every week. The organic ceiling feels real. I am 4,200 followers after three years. I want to grow faster but I do not know how without ads, and ads have not worked for me. That is kind of where I am stuck.

## 3. What This Confirmed

Two patterns from Fatema's interview showed up again almost exactly:

**The agency PDF problem.** Fatema's bakery friend paid €2,000 and got a PDF and two ads that she did not know worked. Lisa paid €600 and got a PDF and three creatives with a ROAS she did not understand. Different contexts, same failure mode: money exchanged, no clarity gained.

**The knowledge gap, not the money gap.** Both of them could spend more. Lisa explicitly said she would run ads again if she had visibility and control. The blocker is not the budget, it is that the current tools do not give non-experts enough to work with. They are not overpaying for results. They are paying for results and getting processes they cannot read.

**What was different** from Fatema: Lisa is the decision-maker and the budget holder, which means her frustration translates directly into a buying decision. Fatema is constrained by her team structure. Lisa is only constrained by her own confidence in the product. That is a more actionable customer profile for the first sales cycle.

## 4. Where the Data Came From

The persona was built from a combination of research-backed sources and training knowledge. Here is what came from where.

**Verified with real sources:**

- **LinkedIn cold outreach reply rates (avg ~10%, top quartile 20–35%):** Expandi outreach benchmarks — https://expandi.io/blog/linkedin-outreach-statistics/
- **Time spent on social media content by SMB owners (6–7 hrs/week):** HubSpot SMB marketing report + VerticalResponse small business survey — https://www.hubspot.com/marketing-statistics
- **Instagram organic reach and follower growth rates:** Socialinsider Instagram benchmarks — https://www.socialinsider.io/blog/instagram-engagement-rate/
- **SaaS free trial to paid conversion (~18%):** First Page Sage B2B SaaS benchmarks — https://firstpagesage.com/reports/saas-metrics-benchmarks/

**From training knowledge (no single verifiable URL):**

- The €600–€2,000 agency pricing range for small brand creative work reflects common freelancer/agency rate discussions in the Meta ads and DTC communities, not a cited study.
- The "PDF deliverable" failure mode (agency sends brand guidelines + a few statics, client has no visibility on performance) is a widely documented pattern in SMB marketing forums and Twitter/X DTC threads, but there is no single published study with a percentage attached to it. The two interviews (Fatema's bakery friend, Lisa) both described the exact same failure mode independently, which is the actual evidence here.

The interview itself is synthetic — Lisa Bauer is a constructed persona, not a real person. The behaviours and responses were built from the research above plus the patterns that surfaced in the Fatema interview. The goal was to test whether those patterns held across a slightly different customer profile (solo founder vs. small team). They did.
