Shortly describe the setting and the audience for your pitch. (e.g., In-class presentation, friendly practice session, formal pitch event).

The pitch was at Better Products, which is this event the module runs where you actually go up and pitch to a real audience. Stage, timer, five minutes to present and then questions from a panel. The room was students and faculty but also people from outside. One of the students in the audience was a marketing director from an actual company (Get Your Guide).


Reflect on giving this pitch. What did you learn from the audience's reactions? (Which parts of your pitch created the most energy or confusion? What did their body language tell you?).

I opened with a story about a friend who runs a bakery. She paid an agency $2,000 and received a PDF and two Instagram ads. She did not even know if they worked. That is the problem Mango Lab is solving. In earlier practice runs I was leading with data, like "70% of SMBs struggle with social media," and nobody moved. The moment I put her on slide one, a specific person on a specific Monday morning, the marketing director in the audience leaned in. I could actually see it happen. She hit a real spot in that story, asked a ton of questions after, and we could answer all of them because we had done the research on this exact pain. The rest of the room kind of followed during the pitch too. The slides I had stripped down to one idea per frame held people in a way the older decks did not. I think I had something like fourteen slides before, a lot of them with five bullet points each. You could see people go somewhere else the second those came up.


Did you iterate your pitch after the experience? If yes, how? (What specific part of your story, a slide, or a key message did you change based on the feedback you received?).

After the first practice run I went back to Guy Kawasaki's stuff, the "problem first" principle. My original version spent slide two explaining what Mango Lab does before the audience even had a reason to care. I flipped it. Lead with the problem: SMB owners are invisible on social not because they do not try but because nobody gave them a playbook. Then the product actually makes sense when it shows up. I also cut the phrase "our platform" from every slide because it does nothing. Replaced it with a before-and-after: here is what Monday looks like without this, here is what it looks like with it. Deck went from fourteen slides to nine.


What was the most challenging or insightful question you received from the audience? What does this question reveal about the biggest risk or the most compelling part of your venture?

The hardest question was "how are you actually going to get your first customers?" I kind of froze on it. The acquisition plan we had at the time was basically organic content and word of mouth, which only works once you already have something going. SMBs are genuinely hard to reach from zero. They are not reading newsletters, they are not showing up to startup events, the sales cycle is slow. I did not have a clean answer and I knew it. That question is more or less what pushed us to rethink the whole acquisition side in the sprint after. Something it had me reflecting on later was the actual cost structure: an SMB that does not already have a budget for ads is not just paying for our platform. It is the cost of our platform plus their ad spend. Right now we are actively trying to solve that with the decoy offer.
