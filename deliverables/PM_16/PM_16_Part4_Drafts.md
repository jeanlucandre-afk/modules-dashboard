## 14. Reflecting on the entrepreneurial stories of other founders that were presenting in the class, which specific lesson or mistake resonates most with your project right now, and why?

> **STATUS: ✅ FINAL — humanised via humanise-text skill (2026-04-30, v2). Built around Richard's "sell-before-building" pivot. Threads back to ENGINE_001 as Jean-Luc's response to that lesson.**

The story that resonates most with me right now is Richard's. He and his team built their entire prototype and MVP first, and then ran into the wall everyone warns you about. They couldn't sell it. The reframe came when he started selling before building. He just ran ads, took payment, then figured out how to deliver. That sequence change rewired how I think about the order of operations for Mango Lab.

What got me was when Richard went deeper. Some of his friends had already hired software engineers, paid them for months, and still hadn't sold a single seat. I sat with that for a minute and realised we're closer to that pattern than I'd been willing to admit. Alberto and I have been building Still for two months. The only money the platform has earned is money the agency made for it. The platform itself hasn't been tested by a single cold buyer.

The action I took from that lesson is the campaign that's running right now. ENGINE_001 is €400 of Meta ads pointed at SMBs, asking them to pay €49 for a trial of a product the agency can deliver manually behind the scenes. If the ads convert, the platform thesis survives. If they don't, no amount of additional engineering on Still is going to fix what the market hasn't asked for. We already had the traction test cards from earlier in the semester telling us the offer worked under controlled conditions. The ads test whether the offer survives contact with cold strangers, which is the real validation we need before we spend another month writing code that nobody asked for.

## 15. Reflect on the question of scaling.

> *Why would you / would you not want to scale this project up? Would you want external funding for it? What might be the upsides and downsides of bootstrapping?*

> **STATUS: ✅ FINAL — humanised via humanise-text skill (2026-04-30, v2).**

I want to scale Mango Lab because the pain we are solving shows up the same way everywhere we look. SMBs spending five to fifty thousand euros a month on ads, whether they are in iGaming, e-commerce, DTC or adjacent verticals, all face the same gap. Agency-quality output is priced for enterprise. DIY tools like AdCreative.ai or Canva produce work without strategy. The middle is empty. Still sits in the affordable and fully automated quadrant of the competitive matrix, and that quadrant currently has no incumbent. If we do not scale into it, someone else will.

The unit economics support scaling on top of the strategic case. Blended LTV is €2,600 against a €49 CAC. That is a 53:1 ratio, well past the 3:1 minimum from Disciplined Entrepreneurship Step 17. Gross margin is 89%. Net profit at 100 subscribers is already €3,013 a month at 23% margin. Every additional Still subscriber also compounds the data the platform learns from, which makes the next subscriber's results better. That kind of curve rewards going faster.

**On funding:** I would take external capital, but only after we have hit the milestones that turn the round into a calculated bet. The trigger conditions: paid-trial conversion rate stable above 2%, the first 50 paying subscribers retained past month 3, and clear evidence that one vertical, iGaming or e-commerce, has been won at scale. With those numbers, a seed round becomes about pouring fuel on a fire we can already point to. Without them, capital arrives before we know what to do with it. That is the version of this story that ends with runway burned on a polished product nobody asked for.

**On bootstrapping (where we are now):**

*Upsides:* Every hour of work is paid for by client revenue, which forces discipline on what we build next. Pricing decisions have to survive contact with people who will actually pay. Co-founders keep equity. We get to build the product the validation demands rather than the product the deck slide demands.

*Downsides:* Speed is bounded by the calendar. We are competing against funded players like AdCreative, Superside and Madgicx who can outspend us on ads and engineering. The market window we are aiming at, the convergence of AI creative quality, open campaign APIs and SMB ad budgets at all-time highs, will not stay open forever. If it closes faster than we expected, bootstrapping costs us position.

The plan is to bootstrap until the metrics earn the round, then raise to scale into the verticals the agency has already validated.

## 16. Reflect on your personal "non-negotiables".

> *Be honest with yourself: what specific things in your life are you truly willing to give up for your venture, and what will you protect at all costs?*

> **STATUS: ✅ FINAL — humanised via humanise-text skill (2026-04-30, v2). Sport context (Costa Rica, Muay Thai national, Taekwondo) opens. Sport itself is on give-up list. Physical health split: floor protected, margins given up. Integrity-with-clients moved to give-up with honest Wizard-of-Oz framing; "truth when asked" stays protected as the line under it.**

I learned what this question actually means before I learned what a startup was. I trained Taekwondo seriously for years and competed nationally in Muay Thai in Costa Rica. The lesson the sport taught me, more than any technique, is that balance is not a permanent state. You go out of balance on purpose for a stretch, a fight camp or a season, knowing the imbalance is the cost of the next level. Then you come back. The hard part is being honest about what you're trading and for how long, and being even more honest about the things you cannot trade without losing yourself.

What I am willing to give up:

- **Predictable income.** I chose agency cash flow over the salaried-job path. The trade is locked in until Still hits €10K MRR.
- **Most of my evenings and weekends for the next 24 months.** The window for Still to take its quadrant is short, and the work to get there doesn't fit inside a 9–5.
- **A lot of small consumption habits.** New gear, eating out, most travel plans. All off the table while every euro is more useful inside the business than out of it.
- **The comfort of not being the person responsible.** Sales, delivery, hiring, payroll, missed deadlines, refunded clients. All of it lands on me. I'm choosing that.
- **The sport itself, for now.** I'm not training competitively right now, and I'm not going to be for a while. Taekwondo and Muay Thai made me the person who can sit through what this venture demands. That's the gift the sport gave me. The trade-off is that I can't keep practicing at the level I want while the business is also asking for everything. When Still is running on its own engine, I come back to the gym.
- **Some of my physical health, on the margins.** Sleep dropping below my preferred eight hours. Meals that aren't planned. The session I skipped this morning because the week broke. The hard floor stays in place. The perfectionist version is on hold.
- **The version of myself that has never lied to a client.** This is the one that costs me most to write down. I built the agency on the rule of no overpromises and no work I wouldn't sign my name to. The Wizard-of-Oz test we are running right now bends that rule. Clients are paying €499 for what they think is an automated platform. The platform exists, but most of the work behind it is me and Alberto in a room. They get the result they paid for, but the mechanism is not what they think it is. I am going to keep doing this until Still catches up to its own promise, and I am owning that this is the trade. The protection on top of it is that we will not let the gap become a fraud. The moment a client asks how it actually works, we tell them the truth.

What I will protect, no matter what:

- **A floor under my physical health.** Sleep enough to think clearly, movement enough that the body keeps working, and food that is not made of coffee. The flex above the floor lives in the give-up list. The floor itself is non-negotiable, because every problem feels harder on five hours of sleep.
- **The relationships that keep me sane.** Family, the people closest to me. I will protect time with them as a deliberate calendar item. The default is they get scheduled first, not last.
- **The truth when a client asks for it.** This is the line under the integrity trade-off above. We will run the Wizard-of-Oz pattern. We will not deny it when asked directly. That is the difference between a validation tactic and a lie.
- **My partnership with Alberto.** Co-founder breakdowns kill more startups than competitors do. The 3-day check-in cadence we wrote into the validation plan is not optional. It's how the company stays insulated from the friction every co-founder partnership produces.

## 17. Which legal entity would you form / have you formed, and why?

> *If you are working with an existing business, which alternative legal structure could be interesting, and why?*

> **STATUS: ✅ FINAL — humanized via humanise-text skill (2026-04-30). Locked decisions: operating informally now → UG in 60 days → GmbH on €25K reserves. Vesting deferred to post-class discussion. Visa angle for Alberto added. No Delaware alternative.**

Mango Lab is operating informally right now. We're keeping it that way until the validation work earns the cost of incorporation. The plan: register a UG (haftungsbeschränkt) in Germany in the next 60 days, then convert to a GmbH once we've built up the €25,000 reserve the conversion requires. The UG bridges that gap. We can start with €1 of share capital and grow into the threshold through the 25% retained-earnings rule, which is more useful than parking €25K in a share account from day one.

Two reasons we're incorporating. The first is liability. Mango Lab runs paid ads inside client Meta accounts and handles creative IP, including AI-generated work where copyright is genuinely grey. Any client dispute or platform ban currently lands on me personally, and Still will need the corporate veil before it scales. The second is Alberto. He's in Germany on a visa and his work authorization isn't fully sorted yet. Holding shares in a company is not the same as being employed by one under German immigration rules, so a UG with both of us as shareholders gives him a clean way to be a co-founder while his work permit catches up.

Berlin is the registered seat. It's where Alberto and I both live and work. German tax law is well-understood by the people we'd hire from, and a German VAT ID simplifies EU B2B invoicing through the reverse-charge mechanism.

Founder split: 50/50 between me (sales, delivery) and Alberto (product, tech). We're going to discuss vesting properly after this class. Current thinking is to add a 1-year vesting clause once we incorporate. We trust each other. The clause is there for when someone outside the two of us starts looking at the cap table, like a future hire or investor.

## 19. Reflect on your journey in learning about how to start a startup. How have your expectations changed, what will you do differently in the future?

> **STATUS: ✅ FINAL — humanised via humanise-text skill (2026-04-30, v2). Added: the class made Jean-Luc act-and-sell instead of just build, reinforced work he'd already started, and taught him to value early clients (even friends or assessments) as proof.**

What I expected coming into this course was a stack of methods to apply: Lean Canvas, the Mom Test, Disciplined Entrepreneurship's 24 steps, Y Combinator's playbook. I assumed founding would feel like applying those tools correctly and the answers would follow. The lesson the course actually taught me is that those tools are scaffolding. They help you ask the right questions. They cannot tell you whether your answer is true. The only thing that does that is putting the answer in front of someone with a wallet.

The clearest version of that lesson came from the Wizard-of-Oz test we ran with the gambling brand client. We had been spinning on Still's product positioning, asking ourselves whether it was a creative tool or a measurement tool, an AI ad maker or a media buyer. Then a paying client used the agency's manual workflow, did not realise it was AI behind the curtain, paid €500, and asked when we could do another batch. That single transaction collapsed weeks of positioning debate. Everything we have written since, including the v3 validation plan, the agency-first decision, the move to €49 paid trials over free ones, comes from internalising that one signal.

The deeper change the class made in me is harder to put into a single sentence. Coming in, my default was to build something and then think about how to sell it. The class kept pushing me to invert that. Sell first, build the rest after the buyer has paid. And not as a theoretical principle. As the actual question to ask on a Tuesday: are you talking to a customer this week, or are you polishing a prototype this week? When the answer was honest, it was almost always the second one. ENGINE_001 exists because that question got asked enough times that I could not avoid the answer.

The other thing the course did was confirm a lot of the work I had already started. The pitch deck, the BMC, the risk ledger, the unit economics, the validation plan, the traction test cards were all in motion when I walked into PM_16. What changed is how I value the early clients we already have. The two paying gambling brands and the friends who tested first drafts of the offer are how the business proves it exists. The course taught me to treat them as the ground I build from.

What I would do differently next time:

- **Start selling sooner.** We had something worth charging for at week three, but I waited until I felt ready, which meant adding polish nobody asked for. Selling earlier would have surfaced the same insights weeks faster.
- **Write the validation plan before writing the deck.** I wrote a pitch deck before the v3 validation plan. The deck told a story I wanted to be true. The plan was the version of that story stress-tested against what actually had to work. The order should have been reversed.
- **Don't treat course materials as the curriculum.** The most useful inputs in this assessment came from outside the syllabus. Hormozi's $100M Offers gave me the decoy trial structure. The Mom Test taught me how to run a customer call without leading the witness. Lenny Rachitsky's newsletter has the line about not settling for meh reactions, which I keep coming back to. The class gave me the vocabulary. The actual learning happened outside the syllabus, in conversations with operators who had run the play before.
- **Optimise for sleep and Alberto check-ins instead of for hours worked.** The 3-day cadence we built into the v3 plan came after a stretch where Alberto and I drifted on assumptions for a week and burned a sprint. The fix was a structural one.

What I am taking forward from this module long after the assessment is graded: agency-first as a validation principle, the €49 decoy offer as a way to filter buyers, the 3-day co-founder check-in, and the discipline of reviewing the conversation log before changing direction. And the bigger one underneath all of it: early clients, even the friends-of-friends and the small ones, are the proof the business is real. They built everything else.
