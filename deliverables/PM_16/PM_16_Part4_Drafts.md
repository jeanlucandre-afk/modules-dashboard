## 15. Reflect on the question of scaling.

> *Why would you / would you not want to scale this project up? Would you want external funding for it? What might be the upsides and downsides of bootstrapping?*

**Draft (edit/personalize):**

I want to scale Mango Lab because the pain we are solving is structural, not local. SMBs spending €5K to €50K a month on ads — across iGaming, e-commerce, DTC, and adjacent verticals — face the same gap everywhere: agency-quality output is priced for enterprise, and DIY tools like AdCreative.ai or Canva produce work without strategy. There is no good middle. Still sits in the affordable + fully automated quadrant of the competitive matrix and that quadrant currently has no incumbent. If we don't scale into it, someone else will.

Scaling matters for a second reason: the unit economics support it. Blended LTV is €2,600 against a €49 CAC — a 53:1 ratio, well past the 3:1 minimum from Disciplined Entrepreneurship Step 17. Gross margin is 89%. Net profit at 100 subscribers is already €3,013/month at 23% margin. Every additional Still subscriber compounds the data the platform learns from, which makes the next subscriber's results better. That is the kind of curve that rewards going faster, not slower.

**On funding:** I would take external capital, but only after we have hit the milestones that turn the round from a leap of faith into a calculated bet. Specifically: paid-trial conversion rate stable above 2%, the first 50 paying subscribers retained past month 3, and clear evidence that one vertical (iGaming or e-commerce) has been won at scale. With those numbers, a seed round becomes about pouring fuel on a fire we can already point to. Without them, capital arrives before we know what to do with it — that's how startups end up running out of runway with a polished product nobody asked for.

**On bootstrapping (where we are now):**
- *Upsides:* Every hour of work is paid for by client revenue, which forces discipline. Pricing decisions have to survive contact with people who will or won't pay. Co-founders keep equity. We can take the time to build the product the validation actually demands — not the product the deck slide demands.
- *Downsides:* Speed is bounded by the calendar. We are competing against funded players (AdCreative, Superside, Madgicx) who can outspend us on ads and engineering. If the market window — the convergence of AI creative quality, open campaign APIs, and SMB ad budgets at all-time highs — closes faster than we expected, bootstrapping costs us position.

The plan, then, is bootstrap until the metrics earn the round, then raise to scale into the verticals the agency has already validated.

## 17. Which legal entity would you form / have you formed, and why?

> *If you are working with an existing business, which alternative legal structure could be interesting, and why?*

> **[YOU FILL: have you already incorporated? UG, GmbH, GbR, sole trader (Einzelunternehmen)? Or is the agency still operating informally? Tell Claude in chat and the answer will be rewritten with that as the starting point.]**

**Draft (assumes nothing is formed yet — edit to match reality):**

The agency is currently operating **[informally / as Einzelunternehmen / GbR / etc.]** while we validate the model and accumulate the first €25K of revenue. The intention is to incorporate as a **UG (haftungsbeschränkt)** in Germany within the next 60 days, and convert to a **GmbH** once cumulative reserves cross the €25,000 threshold the GmbH conversion requires.

The reasoning:
- **Limited liability** is the primary driver. We run paid ads inside client Meta accounts and handle creative IP. The risk surface — copyright issues on AI training data, ad-platform compliance, payment disputes — needs the corporate veil that a UG/GmbH provides and that an Einzelunternehmen does not.
- **UG over GmbH at the start** because the €25,000 minimum capital requirement of a GmbH is real money that is more useful inside the business than locked in a registered-share account. The UG lets us start with €1 of share capital and accumulate the €25K via mandatory 25% retained earnings until the conversion qualifies.
- **Berlin (DE) as the registered seat** because that's where we operate, where Alberto is, and because the German tax and labor framework is well-understood by the founders we are most likely to hire from later. SaaS sold to EU customers with a German VAT ID also benefits from the reverse-charge mechanism — it simplifies invoicing.
- **Founder split** stays 50/50 across Jean-Luc (sales, delivery) and Alberto (product, tech) until a priced round, with vesting cliffs (typically 1-year cliff, 4-year linear) included in the founders' agreement to protect the company from a co-founder leaving in year one. Vesting is not a sign of distrust — it is a sign that we both expect this to last long enough that it matters.
- **Alternative considered:** a Delaware C-Corp would only become attractive if we plan to raise from US VCs in the next round. That decision depends on Day 21 outcomes from the validation sprint. For now, German incorporation matches where the customers, the bank account, and the team actually are.

## 19. Reflect on your journey in learning about how to start a startup. How have your expectations changed, what will you do differently in the future?

**Draft (edit/personalize):**

What I expected coming into this course was a sequence of frameworks — Lean Canvas, the Mom Test, Disciplined Entrepreneurship's 24 steps, Y Combinator's playbook. I assumed founding would feel like applying those frameworks correctly. What changed is realizing that the frameworks are scaffolding, not answers. They are useful for asking the right questions; they cannot tell you whether your answer is true. The only thing that does that is putting the answer in front of someone with a wallet.

The clearest version of this lesson came from the Wizard-of-Oz test we ran with the gambling brand client. We had been agonizing over Still's product positioning — "is it a creative tool or a measurement tool, an AI ad maker or a media buyer?" — and going in circles in the deck. Then a paying client used the agency's manual workflow, didn't realize it was AI, paid €500, and asked when we could do another batch. That single transaction collapsed weeks of positioning debate. The market does not care what we call the product; it cares whether the output works. Everything we've written since — the v3 validation plan, the agency-first decision, the rejection of free trials in favour of €49 paid trials — comes from internalizing that one signal.

What I'd do differently next time:
- **Start selling sooner.** We had something worth charging for at week three, but I waited until I felt "ready" — which meant adding polish nobody asked for. Selling earlier would have surfaced the same insights weeks faster.
- **Write the validation plan before writing the deck.** I wrote a beautiful pitch deck before the v3 plan. The deck told a story I wanted to be true; the plan told me what would make the story actually true. That order should have been reversed.
- **Don't treat course materials as the curriculum.** The most useful inputs in this assessment came from things outside the syllabus — Hormozi's $100M Offers for the trial decoy, Mom Test for the conversation rules, Lenny Rachitsky's newsletter for the "don't settle for meh reactions" post. The course gave me a vocabulary; the operators gave me the verbs.
- **Optimise for sleep and Alberto check-ins, not for hours worked.** The 3-day cadence we built into the v3 plan came after a stretch where Alberto and I drifted on assumptions for a week and burned a sprint. The fix was structural, not effortful.

What I'm taking forward from this module that I will keep using long after the assessment is graded: agency-first as a validation principle, the €49 decoy offer as a way to filter buyers, the 3-day co-founder check-in, and the discipline of reviewing the conversation log before changing direction.
