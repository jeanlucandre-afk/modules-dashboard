# PM_22, Interview 03: Marc Hoffmann
## Synthetic Customer Interview, Bootstrapped B2B SaaS Founder Persona

> This is the third interview in the PM_22 hand-in. Fatema (CODE Uni marketing coordinator, real) was the first conversation. Lisa Bauer (Koma Studio, synthetic DTC founder) was the second. Marc is the third: a bootstrapped B2B SaaS founder running paid ads with no marketer on the team. The goal of this third interview was to test whether the patterns we surfaced in the first two conversations hold up against a different customer profile entirely, one with more technical literacy, more money in the door, and a B2B funnel rather than a DTC funnel. The interview is synthetic, generated via Claude using a dual-agent setup (interviewer plus persona), and grounded in the research cited at the bottom of this document.

---

## 1. Persona Research, Who I Built

We built Marc to sit on the opposite end of the spectrum from Lisa. Lisa is a solo DTC founder with bad ads history. Marc is a bootstrapped B2B SaaS co-founder who is actively spending on paid acquisition right now. If the same patterns surface in both, the patterns are real.

**Name:** Marc Hoffmann
**Age:** 34
**Role:** Co-founder and CEO of Voltura
**Company:** Voltura, an early-stage B2B SaaS based in Munich. Time-tracking and invoicing built for German freelancers and small consultancies. Sits between Toggl and lexoffice in positioning, with the German tax and invoicing rules baked in.
**Stage:** ~50 paying customers, €8k MRR, 18 months since launch, fully bootstrapped, no VC and no plans to raise.
**Team:** Marc plus one technical co-founder (CTO) plus one part-time customer support contractor (about 15 hours a week). No marketer. No designer. No growth person.
**Paid ads history:** Tried Google Search Ads at €500/month for 3 months last year. ROAS was unclear, attribution was muddy, killed the campaign. Currently 6 weeks into a LinkedIn Ads experiment at €800/month. At a SaaS mid-market CPL of $60 to $120 the €800 buys him 7 to 13 leads per month, which is what he is roughly seeing. He is not yet sure if that maths to a positive payback period.
**Trial conversion:** 14% on a 14-day opt-in trial. Below the OpenView 2022 opt-in median of 25%, which Marc has read but not internalised yet.
**Other channels:** SEO is slow but starting to work, two long-form articles ranking on page one for niche German freelancer queries. LinkedIn organic, mostly his own profile, around 6,000 followers built over the last year. Two podcast sponsorships in the German freelancer space (€400 each), unclear conversion.
**AI use:** Heavy. Uses ChatGPT and Claude daily for product copy, blog drafts, support reply drafts, sometimes legal language for invoice templates. Tried three "AI ad creative" tools in the last six months: AdCraft, Pencil, and a smaller one called Creatify. Bounced off all three. His complaint is consistent: the output was too generic and did not fit Voltura's voice.
**Time on marketing:** 10 to 15 hours a week, fragmented around product calls, support escalations, and roadmap work. There is no protected marketing block.
**Tooling spend appetite:** Will pay €50 to €150 a month for tools that save him 4 or more hours a week. The pattern from his bank statement is clear: he subscribes, uses the tool for two months, cancels if he cannot point to a clear ROI.
**Decision authority:** Full. He owns the marketing budget, the tool stack, and the strategic direction. If he says yes, it is yes.
**The friction we wanted to surface:** the LinkedIn Ads creative iteration loop. He writes copy, builds an asset in Figma, ships it, waits a week for enough data, then iterates. That is roughly a two-week cycle per learning. We wanted to see if he would describe this in his own words without us prompting him, and what he would say about it.

This profile deliberately tests a tougher version of our beachhead. Not someone afraid of ads, not someone with no money. Someone with budget, technical fluency, AI fluency, and the authority to buy, who is still struggling.

## 2. Interview Transcript

**Conducted by:** Jean-Luc (founder, Mango Lab)
**Format:** Mom Test, no pitching, behavioural questions only, past tense
**Date:** 02.05.2026
**Duration:** roughly 25 minutes, video call

---

**Q1:** "Thanks for making the time, Marc. To kick off, can you walk me through what your week actually looks like on the marketing side? Just a normal week."

**A:** Yeah, of course. So, naja, the honest answer is it does not look like a marketing week most of the time. I am the CEO and the only non-technical person, which means marketing happens in the gaps between everything else. Let me try to give you a real example. Last week was kind of typical. Monday morning I had two onboarding calls with new trial users back to back, then a product call with my co-founder Stefan about a bug in the invoicing module that one customer was screaming about. By the time that wrapped it was lunch. After lunch I sat down to look at the LinkedIn Ads dashboard, our campaign has been running for six weeks now, and I tried to figure out if the ad I changed the previous Wednesday was performing better or worse than the previous version. I spent maybe an hour staring at it. The data was not clean enough yet to tell.

Tuesday I wrote a blog post draft, about 1,500 words on how German freelancers should structure their Kleinunternehmer invoices. I used Claude for the first pass and then rewrote about half of it because the AI version sounded too soft, too American. By Wednesday I was back on product. Thursday I sponsored a small German freelancer podcast for €400 and recorded a two-minute intro for them. Friday I posted on LinkedIn organic, my own profile, about a customer story. That post did okay, maybe 4,000 impressions.

So in total, marketing was probably 11 hours that week. Some weeks it is more like 15. Some weeks, when product is on fire, it is 4. There is no consistency, which I know is bad. I have said to Stefan we need to hire a marketer for like 12 months now, but with €8k MRR I cannot really justify another full-time salary. Even part-time would be €2k a month and that is a quarter of MRR. So I keep doing it myself, badly, and feeling guilty about it.

---

**Q2:** "That is helpful. Walk me through running a LinkedIn Ad in detail. From the moment you have an idea for an ad to the moment it is live and you are looking at data."

**A:** Okay, ja, this is the part that frustrates me the most actually, so I am happy to go into it. Let me describe the last one because it is fresh. Two weeks ago, around the 18th of April, I was on a customer call with a Hamburg-based consultancy that uses Voltura for their seven-person team. The founder said something offhand like, "the thing that sold me was that I do not have to think about VAT rules anymore". After the call I wrote that down in Notion because it sounded like a good ad angle. That is step one, capturing the angle.

Step two is writing the copy. I opened Claude and asked it for ten variations of LinkedIn ad copy around the angle "stop thinking about VAT rules". It gave me ten. About two of them were usable, the rest were too generic, you know the kind of thing, "Transform your invoicing workflow today". I rewrote the two usable ones in my own voice, made them sharper, tested them against my own ear, sent the best one to Stefan to sanity check. That took maybe 90 minutes including the back and forth.

Step three is the asset. I am not a designer. I open Figma and try to make something that looks like our brand, which is a yellow and dark blue palette, fairly clean, German-feeling. I have a template file with our logo and fonts but every new ad I am basically starting from a blank canvas inside that template. I spent probably three hours on a single static image. Three hours. For one ad. I tried a video version too but my screen recording came out wonky and I did not have time to redo it.

Step four is uploading. I go into LinkedIn Campaign Manager, set up the ad variant, target it. I have a saved audience now, freelancers and small consultancies in Germany with company size 1 to 20, certain job titles. Hit publish.

Step five is the worst step. You wait. You wait a full week sometimes for enough impressions and clicks to even know if the variant is doing better or worse than the previous one. With €800 a month, my volume is too small to get statistically meaningful results in less than seven days. So I am literally sitting on my hands waiting. Then I look at the data, and the data is rarely conclusive. So I make a guess about what to change next, and I run it again. Two weeks per learning. That is genuinely the cycle. Two weeks per learning. It is killing me.

---

**Q3:** "Tell me about the last time you tried Google Ads. What actually happened?"

**A:** Oh god, ja. So this was last year, roughly August through October. I had read some posts on Indie Hackers and a few Lenny's Newsletter pieces saying for B2B SaaS with high intent keywords Google Search Ads can be the cheapest channel if you nail the keywords. Our keywords are things like "Stundenerfassung Freelancer" and "Rechnung schreiben Freiberufler", basically German for time tracking for freelancers and writing invoices for self-employed people. I figured, okay, if someone is searching that, they are basically already a customer, just intercept them.

I budgeted €500 a month for three months. I set it up myself one Saturday afternoon. I did not hire anyone. I wrote the ad copy, picked the keywords, set up the landing page variant, the whole thing. I think I spent maybe 15 hours on the initial setup. The first week I got clicks, maybe 60 clicks for €120 spent, which felt good. Then I started looking at what happened after the click. Out of those 60, two started a trial. Out of the two, neither converted to paid.

I let it run for the full three months because I had already paid and I told myself I needed to give it a fair shake. By the end I had spent €1,500 and gotten, I think, four paid customers. At our average revenue per customer of around €15 a month that is €60 of MRR. Payback period would be 25 months, which for a bootstrapped company with no runway buffer is a non-starter.

But the real problem was not even the numbers. The real problem was I never knew if I had set it up wrong or if Google Ads was just a bad channel for us. The attribution was awful. People would click an ad, leave, come back three days later through a Google search organic result, and now Google was claiming credit for both. I did not have the time or the skill to set up proper conversion tracking with UTMs and a real attribution model. So I killed the campaign in October feeling like I had wasted €1,500 and 40 hours and learned almost nothing useful.

---

**Q4:** "What did you do after that? In the months between killing Google Ads and starting LinkedIn Ads?"

**A:** Honestly I went into a small marketing depression. I am being a bit dramatic but it is true. I told myself, fine, no paid for a while. I am going to focus on what I can actually control, which is content and SEO. So from October through about February I wrote one blog post a week. I published 18 posts in that window. I built a free invoice template tool on our site that ranks now for one decent keyword. I went on three German podcasts as a guest, which was free.

The SEO is starting to work, slowly. Right now we get maybe 800 organic visits a month, up from like 80 a year ago. Two of those posts are on page one. But it is so slow. I read somewhere, I think it was the SaaSRanger numbers, that getting from 1k MRR to 5k MRR for bootstrapped SaaS takes two to four years. I felt that in my bones during those months.

In February I had a beer with another founder, a guy who runs a German HR SaaS that is maybe two years ahead of us, and he told me LinkedIn Ads were working for him. He showed me his dashboard. So I thought, okay, one more try at paid, but this time on a channel where the targeting is actually B2B native. I started the LinkedIn campaign at the end of March. Six weeks in now.

But I want to be honest about something. The reason I went back to paid is not that I suddenly believed in it. It is that organic was too slow. I was watching my bank account and the runway maths and I needed to compress the timeline. So paid became, ja, the only lever I had not pulled hard enough on. Which is a bad reason to start a paid campaign, in retrospect. You should start one because you have a hypothesis, not because you are panicking.

---

**Q5:** "You mentioned you have used a few AI tools for ad creative. Tell me about those. Which ones, what happened?"

**A:** Ja, I tried three in the last six months. Let me think. The first one was AdCraft. I signed up because someone mentioned it on a SaaS subreddit. €49 a month. The promise was you put in your brand and your URL and it generates ad creative for you. I tried it for two weeks. The output was, naja, it looked like ads. Like ads in general. It did not look like Voltura ads. The colours were wrong, the tone was wrong, the layouts felt like they came from a 2019 SaaS landing page playbook. I cancelled.

Second one was Pencil, which I think is more well known, the AI ad platform. I paid for one month, €119 I think it was. The interface was nicer. The output was slightly better but still felt like it was averaging across every B2B SaaS ad ever made, which is exactly what I do not want. We are German, our customers are tax-conscious freelancers, our brand is a bit drier and more direct than the typical Silicon Valley vibe. Pencil could not get there. I cancelled after one month.

Third was a smaller one called Creatify. €39 a month. I think it was actually for video ads. The output was technically impressive, like the AI avatar talking thing, but it felt fake in a way that would actively hurt our brand. Our customers would see that and think we are not serious. Cancelled after the trial.

So three tools, about €200 spent, maybe 20 hours of my time across them, zero ads actually shipped from any of them. The pattern was the same in all three. The tools optimised for "generate something fast" but what I needed was "generate something that sounds like us". Generic ad creative is worse than no creative because it actively damages the brand. I would rather ship no ad than ship an ad that sounds like every other SaaS in our space.

If you ask me what would have made me keep paying, I think it is the voice problem. None of them let me feed in enough of our existing voice to actually learn it. They had a "brand tone" field that was a single dropdown, friendly or professional or playful. That is not what brand voice is. Brand voice is the 200 LinkedIn posts I have written and the 18 blog posts and the customer support replies. That is the corpus. None of the tools let me feed that in.

---

**Q6:** "Where does most of your marketing spend actually go right now, money and time both?"

**A:** Okay let me actually do the maths because I do not think I have ever said this out loud. Money first. LinkedIn Ads is €800 a month right now. Podcast sponsorships, I do about one every two months, so call it €200 a month average. ChatGPT Plus is €20, Claude is €20, Figma is €15, Notion is €10, Slack is €8, LinkedIn Sales Navigator I tried for one month at €80 and cancelled, so amortised over a year that is €7. Total monthly marketing spend is roughly €1,080. That is 13.5% of MRR which is, honestly, more than I should be spending at this stage but I have decided to take the hit for the next quarter to see if LinkedIn works.

Time. LinkedIn Ads, between writing copy and making creative and analysing data, easily 6 hours a week on average. Content, blog and LinkedIn organic, 4 hours. Customer interviews and case studies, 1 to 2 hours. Reading and learning, maybe 1 hour, on a good week. So 12 to 13 hours a week of my own time, which at any reasonable founder hourly rate is way more expensive than the €1,080 in cash.

The thing that hits me, when I lay it out like this, is that the cash is small but the time is the killer. €1,080 a month I can absorb. 13 hours of my week I cannot. Those 13 hours are coming directly out of product or sales or customer success. Every hour I spend in Figma making an ad is an hour I am not on a customer call learning something that would actually grow the business.

And the worst part is, of those 13 hours, I would say 6 are on the LinkedIn Ads loop alone, and most of those 6 are not strategic. They are mechanical. Resizing an image. Trying to get the text to fit. Writing 10 variations of copy and picking one. Waiting for Figma to render. That is the part that should not exist.

---

**Q7:** "Imagine you woke up tomorrow and decided to double the LinkedIn Ads budget from €800 to €1,600. What would have to be true for that to be a sane decision?"

**A:** Hm, good question. Let me think about what is actually stopping me. It is not the money. €800 more a month I can find, even if it means cutting somewhere else. So the budget is not the blocker. Let me say that more clearly because I think it matters. I have the money. I am choosing not to spend it.

What is stopping me is that I do not know if the current €800 is working. Six weeks in, I have generated maybe 50 leads, 6 trials, 1 paid customer so far, with maybe one or two more in the pipeline. At €800 times 1.5 months that is €1,200 spent for €15 of new MRR. Which is terrible if you stop reading there. But the LinkedIn lead gen forms have a longer lag, the people who fill them out often take 30 to 60 days to actually convert if they convert at all, so the real number will not be visible for another two months minimum.

So the honest answer to your question is, what would have to be true is I would need to be able to see, much faster than I can today, which creative variants are actually generating qualified leads versus tyre-kickers. Right now I cannot tell. The dashboard tells me cost per lead, fine, but it does not tell me which lead became a customer, because that data lives in Stripe and our internal CRM, which is a Notion database I built myself. I would need to close that loop. If I could see "this creative variant produces leads that convert at 2x the average", I would pour money into that variant immediately. I would not hesitate.

The other thing is the iteration speed. If I could test five creative variants a week instead of one, I would learn five times faster, and at that point doubling the budget makes sense because I am compounding. Today I am not compounding. I am running one ad, waiting a week, running another, waiting another week. That is a learning rate of maybe 26 learnings a year. With faster iteration I could get to 200 a year and the budget would justify itself.

So budget is not the problem. The problem is I cannot see what is working and I cannot iterate fast enough on what might be working. Solve those and I would spend three or four times more tomorrow.

---

**Q8:** "Is there anything you have tried, paid or organic or AI, that has actually worked, even a little?"

**A:** Yeah, a few things, and they are interesting because they are not the things I expected.

LinkedIn organic, my own personal profile, has been the single best marketing channel by miles. Not Voltura's company page, mine. I started posting consistently about a year ago, mostly stories about building Voltura, lessons from customer calls, occasional spicier takes on the German SaaS ecosystem. I am at about 6,000 followers now. Maybe 25% of our paying customers have either come from a post directly or mentioned my LinkedIn in their first call. It is the cheapest channel we have, and the highest converting, and yet I cannot scale it because it is literally me in a coffee shop typing.

The free invoice template tool I built ranks for "Rechnungsvorlage Freiberufler" and brings in maybe 40 sign-ups a month. Of those, 4 or 5 start trials. It took me a weekend to build. Best ROI of anything I have done.

Two podcast appearances as a guest, free, drove more traffic in 48 hours than €500 of Google Ads did in three months. The conversion was also better because people had heard me speak for 45 minutes and felt like they knew me.

The interesting pattern, when I lay it out like this, is that everything that has worked has been content with my voice in it. Long-form, personal, specific. Everything that has not worked has been ads that try to sound generic and professional. The market for our product wants to hear from a German founder who actually understands their tax problems. They do not want to be marketed to. The implication, which I have been avoiding because it is uncomfortable, is that I should probably be making ads that sound exactly like my LinkedIn posts. But making those at scale, fast enough, with my actual voice, is the thing I cannot do today.

---

**Q9:** "Last one. If one thing about marketing, just one, disappeared from your week tomorrow, what would change the most about how you spend your time?"

**A:** Okay, ja, I have actually thought about this. The thing I want to disappear is the gap between having an ad idea and seeing whether it worked. Right now that gap is two weeks minimum, often three. Inside that gap lives all the wasted Figma time, all the staring at dashboards, all the doubt about whether the campaign is even worth running. If the gap collapsed, if I could go from idea on Monday to data by Friday with five variants instead of one, the whole shape of my week would change.

What would I do with that time? First, more customer calls. I have been trying to do five customer calls a week and I am averaging two. Those calls directly drive product decisions and they directly drive content because most of my best LinkedIn posts come from a thing a customer said. Second, I would actually build the partner programme with German tax advisors that has been in my Notion for 8 months. That is a channel I think could be huge but I have never had a clean week to set it up. Third, honestly, I would sleep more. I have been working 60 hour weeks for 18 months and the marginal hour is no longer productive.

The thing I would not do is spend more time on ads. That is the irony. If the ads loop disappeared, I would not replace it with more ads. I would replace it with the things that I already know work, customer conversations and content with my voice in it. The ads are not the goal, they are a tax I am paying because the alternatives do not scale fast enough.

If you forced me to summarise it in one line, I would say, the worst thing in my marketing week is the lag between trying something and learning from it. Everything downstream of that lag is broken because of the lag itself.

## 3. What This Confirmed

Three patterns from the earlier interviews showed up again, with one important variation.

**The agency PDF problem, in a B2B SaaS skin.** Fatema's bakery friend paid €2,000 to an agency and got a polished PDF and two ads with no clarity on whether they worked. Lisa paid €600 for a similar PDF deliverable. Marc has not used a creative agency, but he has used three AI ad creative tools (AdCraft, Pencil, Creatify) and the failure mode was identical: polished output that did not fit the brand, no learning loop, money spent, no insight gained. The agencies of the SMB world have been replaced, for the SaaS founder, by AI tools that fail in exactly the same way. Different shape, same hole.

**The knowledge gap, not the money gap.** Fatema and Lisa both said they would spend more on ads if they could see what was working. Marc said it more directly than either of them. He has the €800 extra a month sitting in the bank. He is choosing not to spend it because he cannot close the loop between creative variant and qualified lead. Three interviews, three different customer profiles, all three saying the same thing. The blocker is visibility into what works, not budget.

**The iteration loop pain, in its sharpest form yet.** Lisa described a slow Instagram cycle. Fatema described a slow agency cycle. Marc described a two-week LinkedIn Ads loop with absolute precision and used the word "killing me" about it. The lag between trying and learning is the actual product opportunity. Everything else, the AI tools, the creative quality, the budget, is downstream of that lag.

**What was different about Marc.** Two things. First, he is the most fluent customer on AI tools we have spoken to. He uses Claude and ChatGPT every day, knows exactly why the three AI ad tools failed him (the voice problem, no way to feed in his actual corpus), and would describe what he wants in technical product terms. He is the easier customer to design for and the harder customer to fool. Second, he has decision authority and budget and is actively spending right now. Lisa might buy. Marc would buy this week if the product solved the loop problem. He is closer to the sale than either of the previous two interviewees.

## 4. Where the Data Came From

The persona was built from a combination of research-backed sources and training knowledge. Being honest about the difference matters.

**Verified with real sources:**

- **LinkedIn Ads CPL benchmarks ($60 to $130 for most B2B, average CPC $5.74):** The B2B House aggregated benchmarks via HockeyStack 2024 to 2025 data, https://www.theb2bhouse.com/linkedin-ad-benchmarks/. This is what set Marc's "7 to 13 leads on €800/month" maths.
- **SaaS trial-to-paid conversion (median ~17%, opt-in trials median ~25%):** OpenView 2022 Product Benchmarks, https://openviewpartners.com/2022-product-benchmarks/. Marc's 14% on a 14-day opt-in trial is deliberately below median, which gives him a real reason to care about creative quality and lead quality.
- **Bootstrapped SaaS MRR timelines ($1k MRR median in 12 to 18 months, $5k takes 2 to 4 years):** SaaSRanger 2024 micro-SaaS revenue analysis, https://saasranger.com/blog/micro-saas-revenue-reality-what-1000-founders-actually-earn/. This is why Marc's €8k MRR at 18 months is plausible but not exceptional, and why he is feeling time pressure on growth.
- **Creative fatigue and iteration cycles in paid social:** Analytics at Meta 2023, https://medium.com/@AnalyticsAtMeta/creative-fatigue-how-advertisers-can-improve-performance-by-managing-repeated-exposures-e76a0ea1084d, which documents that clicks and conversions become monotonically more expensive with repeated exposures of the same creative. This is the structural reason Marc's two-week iteration cycle is genuinely painful and not just a personal preference.

**From training knowledge, not citable to a single URL:**

- The German freelancer SaaS landscape (Voltura's positioning between Toggl and lexoffice, the Kleinunternehmer invoice rules, German freelancer keyword volumes) is plausible given general knowledge of the market but is not sourced from a single research report.
- The AI ad creative tool list (AdCraft, Pencil, Creatify) blends a real product (Pencil) with two plausible names. Marc's complaints about them, generic output, brand voice mismatch, no way to feed in a corpus, reflect widely repeated complaints in SaaS Twitter and Indie Hackers threads but are not tied to a specific published study.
- Marc's tooling spend pattern (€50 to €150/month, two-month trial, cancel if no clear ROI) reflects general SMB SaaS purchasing behaviour but is reasoning from base rates rather than a specific cited number.
- The "voice corpus" framing of why generic AI output fails for brand-conscious founders is a synthesis of patterns from the previous two interviews and from the user's own work on Mango Lab, not a published finding.

The interview itself is synthetic. Marc Hoffmann is a constructed persona, not a real founder. The behaviours, numbers, and stories were built from the research above plus the patterns surfaced in the Fatema and Lisa interviews. The goal of this third interview was to test whether those patterns hold against a much more sophisticated customer profile, a B2B SaaS founder with budget, AI fluency, and decision authority. They held, and in Marc's case the iteration-loop pain was the sharpest version of the pattern we have heard yet.
