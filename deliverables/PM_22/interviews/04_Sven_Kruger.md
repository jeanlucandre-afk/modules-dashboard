# PM_22, Synthetic Customer Interview 04
## Sven Krüger, Boutique Performance Marketing Agency Owner, Hamburg

> Fourth interview in the synthetic series. After one real conversation (Fatema, the CODE University marketing coordinator) and two synthetic founder-side personas (Lisa Bauer the DTC matcha founder, Marc Hoffmann the bootstrapped B2B SaaS founder), I wanted to flip the angle and pressure-test the same patterns from the agency side. If small DTC brands keep walking away from agencies with a polished PDF and no clarity, what does the agency owner on the other end of that transaction actually see, do, and worry about? Sven was built to answer that. Disclosed as synthetic, allowed under the brief.

---

## 1. Persona Research, Who I Built

Before the interview I built Sven from three sources: agency retainer benchmarks for small DACH shops, public commentary on agency churn and creative bottlenecks, and the inverse of the patterns Lisa and Fatema surfaced (i.e. who is on the other side of the table when those founders pay and feel let down).

**Name:** Sven Krüger
**Age:** 41
**Business:** Krüger Performance, a small Hamburg-based performance marketing agency. Meta Ads and Google Ads specialists, no SEO, no creative-only work. 8 active retainer clients plus 2–3 project clients per quarter, all SMBs across DACH.
**Founded:** 6 years ago. Sven started as a freelancer after leaving an in-house ecommerce role, slowly added staff.
**Team:** 4 people total. Sven plus 2 ad managers (one mid-level, one junior, both Hamburg) plus 1 designer who works remote from Lisbon.
**Revenue:** ~€340k/year. Average retainer €2,800–€4,500/month per client. Sits in the boutique band, slightly below US comparables (Foxwell Digital benchmarks: US boutique $1,500–$3,000/mo, EU 15–20% below; Sven sits at the upper end of that EU band because DACH SMBs have larger ad budgets).
**Margin:** roughly 25–30% after wages, software, and his own draw. He does not talk about margin in those terms but it shows up in his answers.
**Software spend:** ~€800/month total. Meta Business Suite Pro features, a creative asset library (Frontify-equivalent), reporting tool (Whatagraph-equivalent), the Adobe suite for the designer, plus a handful of smaller tools.
**Decision authority:** full. He is the owner.
**AI usage:** personal ChatGPT for client emails and proposals. Team has no AI workflow. Tried one AI creative tool a year ago and abandoned it.
**Churn:** loses roughly 3 clients a year, mostly at the 12–18 month mark. Aligns with Focus Digital 2024 finding that small agencies (1–10 staff) see 32% annual churn.

This profile sits deliberately in the "successful but stretched" zone. Sven is not failing. He is also not scaling. He is stuck at four people because the unit economics of adding a fifth, without changing how the work gets done, do not pencil. That tension is the interesting one for our pattern-testing.

## 2. Interview Transcript

**Conducted by:** Jean-Luc (founder, Mango Lab / Still)
**Format:** Mom Test, past behaviour only, no pitching
**Date:** 02.05.2026
**Duration:** ~25 minutes
**Language:** English (Sven's choice, fluent with German word-order tells)

---

**Q1:** "Thanks for jumping on. Could you walk me through what a normal week looks like running the agency? Not a special week, just a Tuesday-to-Tuesday."

**A:** Sure, sure. Honestly the week is shaped by two things, the client calls and the creative pipeline, and everything else fits around those.

Monday morning I do internal first. We have a stand-up at nine, the two ad managers and me, the designer joins from Lisbon. We go through every account, what is running, what is paused, what needs new creative, what the numbers did over the weekend. Then I block out the rest of the morning for client emails. The weekend always produces a backlog, clients sending screenshots on Sunday night, "why is this campaign down", and I have to either answer or queue it for Tobias, my senior ad manager.

Tuesday and Wednesday are mostly client calls. With 8 retainer clients that is at minimum 8 monthly review calls, plus 4 or 5 check-ins, plus prospect calls. Tuesday I might have 5 calls back to back. I block Wednesday afternoon for "deep work" but honestly half the time it gets eaten by something urgent.

Thursday is the day I try to actually look at the accounts myself. Tobias and Lina, my junior, run the day-to-day, but I check in on every account at least weekly. And then Thursday afternoon is when I push the designer for the next week's creative batch. That is genau the bottleneck conversation, every Thursday.

Friday is admin and proposals. By Friday afternoon I am usually toast. It is six years of this. The week does not feel like running an agency, it feels like I am the operations valve that everything has to pass through. If I am sick for two days the whole thing wobbles. That is the truth of being four people. There is no real layer between me and the work.

---

**Q2:** "Tell me about your last new client onboarding, from the day they signed to the day the first ad went live. What actually happened?"

**A:** Yeah, this is a good one because it just finished. We signed a Munich SaaS company, B2B project management tool, three weeks ago. Their first ad went live last Wednesday. Three weeks, almost exactly, which is our standard.

Day one after signing we send the welcome pack and the access requests. Meta Business Manager, Google Ads, GA4, their CRM if they have one, brand assets, past creative, past campaign data. That alone takes a week because clients always send half of it and then I have to chase. The Munich SaaS, they were quite organised, they sent most of it within four days. Unusually good.

Week one is also brand discovery. We do a 90-minute kickoff call where we go through positioning, audience, past performance, the competitive set. I take notes and Lina writes up the creative brief. The brief is what the designer works from.

Week two is creative production. The designer is in Lisbon, does everything in Figma and After Effects, and the queue is always full. When the Munich brief lands on his desk, he already has work for three other clients in front of it. We try to push priority, but realistically he gets to it in the second half of week two. For a new client's first batch, maybe 8 to 12 statics and 3 to 4 short videos. That is the minimum to actually launch and have variants to test.

Week three is review and revision. Client looks at the creatives, gives feedback, designer revises, client approves. Then we set up campaigns in Meta and Google, audiences, tracking, conversion events. We launch usually on a Wednesday so we have two days of data before the weekend.

So that is three weeks. Sometimes four if the client is slow on assets or feedback. The painful thing is the client is paying us for those three weeks, €3,800 in their case, and they have nothing running. They are paying for setup. Some clients understand this, some get nervous. Every onboarding I am managing the nervousness as much as I am managing the work.

---

**Q3:** "You mentioned the designer is the bottleneck. Walk me through his actual week. What does he do day to day?"

**A:** Mathias, his name. He is excellent, by the way. I do not want to make it sound like he is the problem. He is the most important person in the agency. But yes, the bottleneck is real.

His week, Monday he joins our stand-up, he gets the priority list. We use ClickUp for the queue. At any given moment there are 10 to 15 creative requests open. Some are new client batches, some are iterations on running ads, some are seasonal updates, some are landing page assets. He works through them roughly in priority order, but priority shifts during the week because a campaign tanks and we need new creative urgently, and that jumps the queue.

A realistic day for Mathias is, he produces maybe 4 to 6 finished static creatives, or 1 to 2 short video edits, or 2 to 3 landing page sections. That is one person, one day, working hard. He is fast. But across 8 retainers plus project work, the demand is something like 60 to 80 creative deliverables a month, and he can produce maybe 50 to 60 if nothing goes wrong. So the queue is permanently 10 to 15 deep and we are always behind by about a week.

What this means in practice is, when an ad manager wants to test a new angle, the lead time is a week. Sometimes more. By the time the creative arrives, the campaign context has shifted. The "fresh idea" Tobias had on Monday might not be the most urgent thing by next Tuesday. There is this constant decay of relevance because the creative cannot keep up with the velocity of the ad accounts.

We have talked about hiring a second designer. The maths does not work yet. A good mid-level designer in Hamburg or Berlin is €4,500–€5,500 a month all in, and to cover that I need two more retainers, which means I need to win them and onboard them, which loops back to the designer being overloaded for those onboardings. Chicken and egg. We tried a freelancer in Poland for three months last year. Quality was inconsistent, Mathias spent half his time fixing the work, clients noticed. We stopped.

So yes. Mathias is the constraint. If you ask me what the agency is fundamentally limited by, it is creative throughput. Not media buying skill, not strategy, not client acquisition. Creative.

---

**Q4:** "Tell me about a client you lost in the last year. What actually happened?"

**A:** Okay. The most painful one was a Berlin streetwear brand. We had them for sixteen months. Started at €3,200 a month, grew them well, ROAS sat around 2.8 most of the time, which for fashion is decent. Median Meta ROAS in the Triple Whale benchmarks is around 2.0, so we were well above. They went from €40k a month in revenue to €110k a month over the time we worked together.

What happened was, in month fourteen they hired a head of growth. Late twenties, came from a bigger DTC. Within two months he had built a case internally that they should bring it in-house. "I can run this for half what we pay the agency, and I will have full control over creative." The founder loved that pitch because the founder was always slightly uncomfortable with us. Not because we did anything wrong, but because we were a black box to him. He saw the monthly report, he saw the revenue, but he did not feel close to the work.

The head of growth was right and wrong. He could run it for less in pure cash. What he did not factor in was creative production. We were producing maybe 15 creatives a month for them. So in their first three months in-house, ad performance dropped. ROAS went to 1.6. They were not making new creatives fast enough and the existing ones fatigued. There is good Meta research on creative fatigue, the Analytics at Meta team published on it.

Did we get them back? No. The founder went silent. I think pride. Six months later they hired a different agency, smaller than us, cheaper, in Leipzig.

The pattern is, we lose clients between month twelve and month eighteen, almost without exception. Six months in they are happy because the numbers are improving. Twelve months in they ask why they are paying us so much. Eighteen months in they have either rationalised the cost, found someone cheaper, or built an internal team. We are basically a transitional supplier. They use us until they think they can do it themselves.

---

**Q5:** "You said they often go in-house or to someone cheaper. Of the three you lost last year, where did each of them actually end up?"

**A:** Three lost clients last year, yes. The streetwear brand we just talked about, in-house first, then a smaller agency.

The second was a Vienna supplements brand. They went to a cheaper freelancer, someone on Upwork charging €1,200 a month. The founder told me almost apologetically. "Sven, we love you, but we just need someone to push the buttons, we cannot justify €4,000." Six months later their Instagram was a mess. I do not know if they are still in business.

The third was a Stuttgart B2B services company. They stopped running ads completely. Decided performance marketing was not the right channel and they should invest in content and SEO and outbound sales. Fair conclusion, actually. We had told them their LTV was high and the sales cycle was long, and Meta was probably the wrong tool. They went to a content agency. I respect the decision.

So of the three, one went in-house, one went cheaper, one switched channel. None went to a better agency. That is interesting. People frame agency churn as competitive, like you lose to a better competitor. In my experience you almost never lose to a better competitor. You lose to "we will do it ourselves", "we will do it cheaper", or "we will do something else". The fee-compression pressure is enormous. Every year clients ask for more for less.

---

**Q6:** "Earlier you mentioned trying an AI creative tool. What did you try, what happened?"

**A:** Yeah, about a year ago. I will not name the tool, but it was one of the well-known generative ad creative platforms. The pitch was you upload your brand assets, it generates dozens of variants, you pick the winners, ship them.

I paid for a month, around $200. I sat with Mathias for a morning and we put one of our clients into it, a Munich beauty brand. Uploaded their logo, fonts, product photography, brand colours.

The output was technically functional. It produced maybe 40 variants in twenty minutes. But every single one looked obviously AI-generated. The text placement was slightly wrong, the proportions felt off, the layouts had this generic look you see on bad SaaS landing pages. If I had shown those creatives to the client, the client would have been embarrassed. The whole reason they pay us is so their ads look like a real brand, not like everyone else's.

Mathias was actually fine with it as a starting point. He said, "I could use the layout ideas as inspiration, then redo it properly." But that means it is an inspiration tool, not a production tool. It does not solve my bottleneck. The bottleneck is finished creative, not ideas.

I cancelled the subscription. I have not tried another one since. The model quality has improved, I know, but I have a high bar now. If I cannot show the output to a paying client without a human polishing it for an hour, it is not saving me time, it is shifting the work.

What I personally use ChatGPT for is the boring stuff. Client emails when I am tired. First drafts of proposals. Summarising a long client call into action items. That saves me an hour a day. But for creative production, no. Not yet.

---

**Q7:** "Hypothetical, but grounded. If Mathias doubled his output tomorrow, same quality, what would actually change for the agency?"

**A:** Okay, this is a fun one because I have thought about it. Honestly, a lot.

First thing, immediately, the queue clears. The constant pressure of being a week behind, gone. That changes the feel of the agency. Right now there is always a low-level stress, "are we behind on the X account, do we have creatives ready for Friday's launch, did the brief get to Mathias". If that pressure disappeared the team would be much happier, and I would sleep better.

Second, we could test more aggressively. Right now Tobias and Lina pick their battles on testing because they know each new test costs creative. If creative was effectively free, or doubled, they would test more variants, more angles, more audiences. Performance would go up across all clients. I would estimate maybe 15 to 25% improvement in average ROAS just from being able to test more. That is huge for client retention.

Third, and this is the real answer, I could take on more clients without hiring. Right now we are at 8 retainers and that is genau the ceiling for our current creative throughput. If creative doubled, I could probably take 12 retainers with the same team. At an average of €3,500 each that is an extra €14,000 a month in revenue with almost zero additional cost. That is the real prize.

Fourth, onboarding gets faster. Two of those three weeks are creative production. If creative is twice as fast, onboarding goes from three weeks to ten days. The "we are paying you and nothing is running" period is the most fragile moment in the relationship.

Now, the honest counter-question. If a tool actually doubled creative output at the same quality, it would not stay an agency-only tool. Clients would find it themselves. The Berlin streetwear founder, his next move would be to hand that head of growth the tool. Then the question becomes, what is the agency for? If creative is no longer the bottleneck, agency value drops. You are paying us for buying expertise and strategy, which is real, but it is harder to charge €3,500 a month for than "we make all your ads".

So I have a complicated relationship with this hypothetical. I want it for the agency. I am also slightly afraid of what it does to the agency model.

---

**Q8:** "Where does most of the agency software budget go, and what is actually pulling its weight?"

**A:** Total software spend is around €800 a month. I just did this exercise for tax.

Adobe Creative Cloud for Mathias, around €60 a month, essential, the tool he lives in. The reporting tool is around €200 a month, we use it to produce client reports automatically pulling from Meta and Google. Before this Lina built reports manually in Google Slides, took half a day per client per month. Now it is twenty minutes. Worth it.

The creative asset library, brand assets and archive, about €120 a month. Less essential. We could probably do it in Google Drive but the search is much better. Meta Business Suite premium features, advanced reporting, custom audiences at scale, around €150 a month. Then smaller tools, ClickUp, Loom, Calendly, a transcription tool, Slack, maybe €200 a month combined.

What is pulling its weight, the reporting tool and Adobe. Those two I would never cut. The reporting tool gives Lina half her week back. Adobe, Mathias literally cannot work without it.

What is mediocre, the asset library. I could replace it with something cheaper.

What I would pay for if it existed, and this is genuinely something I think about, is a tool that compresses creative production time without compromising quality. I would pay €500, maybe €1,000 a month for that, easily. The problem is the AI tools I have seen so far either compromise quality or do not actually compress time, they just shift it. If someone solved that I would write the cheque on the spot. But I have been burned enough that I would want to test it carefully.

---

**Q9:** "Last one. If you had to bet on one change in the next two years that would reshape boutique agencies like yours, what would it be?"

**A:** One change. Hmm.

I do not think it is "AI replaces agencies". I have heard that pitch for two years now and the model quality is still not there for production work, and even if it were, the strategy and the relationship and the buying expertise still matter. That is not it.

The real change is that the line between "agency client" and "agency-equipped in-houser" is going to blur. Right now there are two clear modes. Either you are a small brand and you hire an agency, or you are bigger and you have an in-house team. The agency relationship is opaque, the in-house one is transparent. My bet is that in two years, the small brand will expect agency-level work delivered with in-house-level transparency. They will want to see the work, the variants, the decisions, the way an in-house team would. The PDF-deliverable model is dying.

This is uncomfortable for agencies because it requires us to either become more transparent, which exposes how the sausage gets made, or to charge more for the opacity, which compresses against cheaper-freelancer pressure. I have started doing more of this myself. Weekly Loom videos for two of my clients, walking them through the account. They love it. Takes me 45 minutes a week per client. Does not scale.

The other change, related, is that the smartest small brands will buy tools, not services. The Berlin streetwear brand, if there had been a tool a year ago that gave them what we gave them at a fraction of the cost, they would have used it. Those tools are starting to exist. Not great yet. In two years they will be much better.

So my bet is, the boutique agency in two years has to do one of two things. Either go upmarket, become genuinely strategic, charge €8,000 a month, work with bigger clients who want a partner. Or go niche, the best in the world at one specific thing, Meta Ads for sleep brands or Google Ads for B2B SaaS, charge a premium for specialisation. The middle, where I sit right now, generalist boutique, four people, eight retainers, that middle gets squeezed from both sides. From above by the strategic agencies, from below by the cheap freelancers and the tools. That is what keeps me up.

## 3. What This Confirmed

Three patterns to reflect on. Each one connects to an earlier interview and either confirms, complicates, or extends what we already saw.

**The agency PDF problem, viewed from the other side.** Lisa described paying €600 to a freelancer and receiving a polished PDF and three creatives she could not read. Fatema's bakery friend paid €2,000 for a similar deliverable. Sven's Q9 answer described exactly this failure mode from the agency side without being prompted. He called it "the PDF-deliverable model" and predicted it is dying. Importantly, Sven does not see himself as the agency in Lisa's story. He frames himself as someone trying to escape that model with weekly Loom videos for two clients. That is genuine, but it is also expensive (45 minutes a week per client) and selective (only two of his eight retainers get it). The honest read is that Sven is one tier above the freelancer who gave Lisa the PDF, but the structural pressure on his model is the same. He is one cost-pressure decision away from being that freelancer. He knows it.

**The designer bottleneck dominates the week.** This was the strongest signal in the interview, and it confirmed something I suspected from Lisa's frustration about ad creatives "not looking like Koma". On the founder side, the complaint is "the creative did not feel like my brand". On the agency side, the answer is "creative throughput is the constraint of the entire business, and we use one designer trying to keep up with 8 retainers". The same shortage produces both pains. Sven's Q3 and Q7 answers were unprompted and specific (4–6 statics or 1–2 videos per day, queue 10–15 deep, hiring a second designer does not pencil). The interesting follow-up: a tool that meaningfully expands creative throughput for Sven would also help Lisa directly, because Lisa is doing her own creative anyway. Same wedge, two angles.

**Tool versus competition is the real tension.** Sven's Q7 answer was the most revealing. Unprompted, he articulated that a tool that doubles creative throughput would not stay agency-only, the clients would find it, and the value of an agency drops. He still wants the tool ("I would write the cheque on the spot") but he sees the threat clearly. This complicates the framing that agencies are buyers of throughput tools. They are buyers only if the tool stays inaccessible to clients. If it becomes accessible to clients, the agency is a transitional buyer at best, possibly an obstacle to the tool's adoption. The right early-customer profile is probably not Sven. It is Lisa, or the in-house head of growth at the streetwear brand. Sven is a useful informant, not the wedge.

What is different from Fatema and Lisa: Sven is the only one of the four interviewees who has direct competitive concerns about the kind of tool we are building. Fatema and Lisa want the problem solved at any cost. Sven wants it solved if it benefits him before his clients. That window is narrow.

## 4. Where the Data Came From

The persona and the responses were built from a mix of cited research and training-knowledge patterns. Honest about both.

**Verified with real sources:**

- **Agency retainer benchmarks (boutique $1,500–$3,000/mo US, EU 15–20% below):** Foxwell Digital 2023, https://www.foxwelldigital.com/blog/what-paid-social-agencies-charge-a-guide
- **Small agency churn (32% annual for 1–10 staff, retainer clients stay ~56 months on average across all sizes, but small agencies churn faster):** Focus Digital 2024, https://focus-digital.co/average-marketing-agency-churn/
- **Creative fatigue from repeated ad exposure:** Analytics at Meta 2023, https://medium.com/@AnalyticsAtMeta/creative-fatigue-how-advertisers-can-improve-performance-by-managing-repeated-exposures-e76a0ea1084d
- **Meta ROAS benchmarks (median 2.04, half of accounts below 2:1):** Triple Whale 2024, https://www.triplewhale.com/blog/facebook-ads-benchmarks
- **Agency PDF-deliverable failure mode:** documented qualitatively in the Fatema interview (real) and the Lisa interview (synthetic), both surfacing the same pattern independently.

**From training knowledge (no single citation):**

- Designer day-rate output (4–6 statics or 1–2 videos per finished day at agency-quality) is a working figure based on common DACH agency commentary and Figma/Adobe production benchmarks. There is no single authoritative published figure.
- Mid-level designer all-in cost in Hamburg/Berlin (€4,500–€5,500/month) reflects DACH employer cost discussions in design hiring forums. Approximate, not cited.
- Software-stack composition for a four-person agency (Adobe + reporting + asset library + Meta Suite + project tools = ~€800/month) is built from the typical small-agency stack, not a single survey.
- The "agency loses to in-house, cheaper freelancer, or channel switch, almost never to a better agency" framing is a pattern from agency-owner commentary, supported by the Focus Digital churn data but not directly cited.

The interview is synthetic. Sven Krüger is a constructed persona, not a real person. The behaviours, numbers, and stories were generated to be internally consistent with the cited research and to pressure-test the patterns surfaced in the Fatema and Lisa interviews from the opposite side of the transaction.

## Methodology footer

Synthetic interview, generated via Claude dual-agent process (interviewer prompt and persona prompt run separately, edited for flow). Grounded in cited Foxwell Digital, Focus Digital, Analytics at Meta, and Triple Whale research. Pattern-tested against the real Fatema interview and the synthetic Lisa Bauer interview. Disclosed as synthetic in the brief. Not represented as a real customer conversation.
