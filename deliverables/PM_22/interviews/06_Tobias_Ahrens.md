# PM_22, Synthetic Customer Interview 06
## Tobias Ahrens, B2B Sales-Training Consultant, Cologne

> This is the sixth interview in the PM_22 discovery set and the second synthetic one I built to pressure-test the edges of the beachhead. Lisa, Marc and Anja all sit comfortably inside e-commerce or B2B SaaS. Tobias does not. He is a solo consultant. His "creative" is not an ad image, it is a 12-page PDF lead magnet. The question I wanted answered is whether the iteration-loop pain Marc and Anja described shows up in his work in the same shape, or whether the shape changes enough that Still has to make a strategic call about whether to serve service businesses at all. Disclosed: Tobias is a constructed persona, grounded in cited research and the patterns from the Fatema, Lisa, Marc and Anja conversations.

---

## 1. Persona Research, Who I Built

The persona is built from three layers: patterns from the previous five interviews, public benchmarks for LinkedIn-driven B2B lead generation, and the standard tooling stack used by solo consultants in the DACH region.

**Name:** Tobias Ahrens
**Age:** 38
**Business:** Sole-proprietor B2B sales-training consultant in Cologne. His clients are mid-market German Mittelstand companies, mostly SaaS and industrial software, with sales teams between 5 and 30 people.
**Offering:** A 6-week online program "Enterprise Sales Lab" at €2,400 per seat, plus 1:1 coaching at €350 per hour.
**Revenue:** ~€140k per year. Roughly 75 percent from program seats.
**Marketing engine:** LinkedIn organic, three posts per week, 12,000 followers built over four years. He runs LinkedIn Ads to a free 12-page PDF on enterprise sales, then nurtures via a 7-email sequence.
**Spend:** €600/month LinkedIn Ads, €99/month email tool, €30/month landing page builder, plus Calendly, Zapier, Otter and Notion.
**Lead economics:** at the going B2B Lead Gen CPL of $60 to $120, his €600 buys ~5 to 10 leads/month, feeding 1 to 2 program conversions/month at €2,400 each. Considered Meta, decided his audience was on LinkedIn.
**AI usage:** heavy. Drafts every LinkedIn post in Claude and rewrites it. Otter for client calls. Calendly plus Zapier for booking and onboarding.
**The pain:** the lead magnet is the bottleneck. Every new angle means a new PDF, a new landing page and a new 7-email sequence. The copy takes him an afternoon. The design takes three weeks.
**Decision authority:** complete. Budget holder, marketer and deliverer.

This profile sits deliberately at the edge of the beachhead. He has the same iteration-loop frustration Marc and Anja described, but the surface where it lives is a lead-magnet funnel, not an ad-creative library. If the underlying pain is the same, that is a strong signal Still's wedge can stretch. If the pain is shaped differently enough, that is a strategic boundary line.

## 2. Interview Transcript

**Conducted by:** Jean-Luc (founder, Mango Lab / Still)
**Format:** Mom Test, no pitching, past-tense behavioural questions
**Date:** 02.05.2026
**Duration:** 25 minutes

---

**Q1:** "Thanks for making time, Tobias. Can you walk me through what your week looks like on the marketing side? A normal week, what are you actually doing?"

**A:** Sure. The way I think about it, there are basically three buckets, and marketing is one of them, sitting alongside delivery and admin. Delivery is cohort calls or 1:1 coaching, roughly half my time. Admin is invoices, scheduling, email. Marketing is the third bucket, probably 12 to 15 hours a week, which is a lot when you say it out loud.

The bulk of those hours is LinkedIn. Three posts a week, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, because that is when my audience is on the platform. Each post takes me roughly 90 minutes. I open Claude, dump in a rough idea or a story from a recent client call, ask for a first draft, then I rewrite probably 70 percent of it because the AI version sounds like an AI version. Then I find or shoot a photo, write the hook three times, read it out loud, post.

Then there is the engagement tax, the first hour after a post goes live. I sit there replying to comments, because if I do not engage in the first hour the post just dies. Another 90 minutes per posting day. Three posts a week, that alone is nine hours.

Then the ads side, smaller time commitment but a bigger source of stress. I check the LinkedIn Ads dashboard four or five times a week. I am not changing things constantly, I have learned that lesson, but I look at cost per lead and at which audience is performing. Maybe an hour a week.

The rest is funnel work. Updating the landing page when the copy is stale, tweaking the email sequence. That is the bucket I keep underestimating. I always think it will take an afternoon and it never does.

Honestly, if I had to name the one thing that drains me, it is not the content. The content is the part I am good at. It is the funnel work.

---

**Q2:** "Tell me about the lead magnet. What is it, how did it come together, what does the funnel actually look like end to end?"

**A:** The lead magnet is a 12-page PDF called "The Enterprise Sales Operating Manual." The pitch on the landing page is: if you are selling into mid-market German companies and your average deal size is over 50k, here are the seven mistakes I see sales teams making, with a framework for fixing each one. It is a real document. I spent probably 60 hours writing the first version, because I wanted something that, even if someone only ever read this PDF, they would still get value.

The funnel itself is standard. Someone sees a LinkedIn ad, they click, they land on a Carrd page, they put in their email and their company, and they get the PDF in their inbox within 30 seconds, automated through Zapier into my email tool. Then the 7-email sequence kicks off over 14 days. Email one is the PDF. Email two is a case study. Email three is a deeper dive on one of the frameworks. Email four is a soft pitch. Five and six are more value. Seven is the harder pitch with a Calendly link.

Conversion-wise, the landing page converts at 35 to 40 percent, which I am happy with. Email open rates start around 55 percent for email one and drop to about 28 percent by email seven, which is also normal. About 6 percent of people who download book a Calendly call. About a third of those calls turn into program seats. So 10 leads in a month is roughly 0.6 calls and 0.2 closed deals, which is too small to be predictable. To make the maths work I need 25 to 30 leads a month, which I do not have.

How it came together: the first version, in 2023, took ten weeks total. Two weeks of writing, three weeks of design because I was paying a freelancer in Lisbon to lay it out in InDesign, two weeks of building the landing page, three weeks for the email sequence. After that, every time I have wanted to change the angle or test a new hook, I have had to redo basically all of it. New PDF, new landing page, new emails. That is the part that has become unworkable.

---

**Q3:** "The last time you wanted to test a new lead-magnet angle, what happened?"

**A:** Yeah, this is fresh. End of January I had what felt like a really clear insight. The current PDF is positioned around enterprise selling in general. I noticed almost all of my best fit prospects were dealing with deal cycles longer than four months, with very specific pain around losing momentum mid-cycle. So I thought: a second lead magnet, much narrower, called something like "Closing the Six-Month Deal," parallel campaign for just that segment. Same funnel structure, different angle.

I sat down on a Sunday and wrote the whole PDF copy in about five hours. Wrote the new landing page copy on Monday morning, maybe 90 minutes. Drafted the seven-email sequence on Monday afternoon. By Monday evening I had the entire intellectual content of a new funnel ready. I felt great. I told myself I would have it live by end of the week.

I am telling you this in May. It is still not live.

The PDF design is the bottleneck. I sent the copy to my Lisbon freelancer in early February. She came back two weeks later with a first layout, but the cover did not match what I wanted, the icons were generic stock, the typography did not feel like my brand. Two revision rounds, until mid-March. Then she went on holiday for ten days. Then I noticed the call-out boxes did not work on mobile. Another revision round. By early April I had a PDF I was happy with.

Then the landing page. I use Carrd, fine for the existing page, but I wanted something with a video header, so I tried Framer myself. Four evenings and it still does not feel right. Sitting in draft for three weeks. The emails I have not even started loading into the email tool, because I told myself I would do that once the PDF and landing page were done.

So a Sunday afternoon of writing has become 14 weeks of asset production, and the campaign I had a clear hypothesis about in January, I have not been able to test. By the time I launch, my insight will be five months old. That is the part that drives me a little crazy.

---

**Q4:** "Talk me through the gap between writing the copy and getting the asset live. Where exactly does the time go?"

**A:** If I break the 14 weeks down honestly, the actual creative work, the thinking, the writing, the strategic decisions, that is maybe 12 hours total. The rest is asset production friction.

There is the freelancer cycle, the biggest chunk. I write a brief, she works for a week or two, I review, I write feedback, she revises, I review again. Each cycle is seven to ten days of calendar time and maybe an hour of my actual hands-on time. The calendar time is the thing that kills me, not the hours.

There is the iteration cost. If I want to change the headline on the cover, that is a new file, a new export, possibly new spacing on the page. A change that takes me 30 seconds in my head takes her 20 minutes and then a day of back and forth. So I have learned to batch my feedback, which is good for her but means I am sitting on revisions for days before I send them.

Then the landing page, which I do myself, which means it is faster but also competing for attention with everything else in my week. Client calls, program delivery, LinkedIn posts. The landing page slips. Every. Single. Time.

And then there is the "is this good enough" anxiety. The lead magnet is the front door of my business, so I have a hard time saying "fine, ship it" when I know it is not as polished as the existing one. I cannot tell you how many hours I have spent moving an icon two pixels.

The honest summary: the actual creative thinking is maybe 5 percent of the total elapsed time. The other 95 percent is production, coordination and second-guessing. That ratio is upside down.

---

**Q5:** "Tell me about the LinkedIn Ads. When did you start running them, what was the first month like?"

**A:** I started in May 2024, almost exactly a year ago. Before that I had been pure organic for two years and had hit a ceiling. 8,000 followers, decent engagement, but maybe three program enquiries a month from organic and I needed five or six.

The first month was painful and instructive. €500 budget, Lead Gen Form campaign, because I had read Lead Gen Forms convert better than off-platform landing pages. Targeting: sales leaders at companies with 50 to 1,000 employees in DACH. Existing PDF as the offer.

First week, CPL about $180, well above the benchmark range of $50 to $130 for B2B Lead Gen Forms. Two leads for €200 of spend, and the leads were not qualified, lots of junior SDRs, not the decision-makers I sell to. So I narrowed targeting and the CPL went up to about $220. Worse.

Then I read a thread suggesting that for premium B2B you actually want broader targeting and let LinkedIn's algorithm find the qualified people. CPL came down to $95, in the range, but lead quality was a mixed bag. Some great, some students.

Where I landed by month three was a CPL around $80 to $110, maybe 6 leads a month for €600 of spend, which is roughly the picture today. Of those 6, about 1 books a call, about a third close. €600/month buys me 0.2 deals at €2,400, which is €480 of revenue. Net of ad spend, negative. It makes economic sense because a percentage of program graduates buy 1:1 coaching, so lifetime value is closer to €4,000.

But the headline: I have spent over €7,000 in a year and the unit economics are tight. The only real lever is improving the lead magnet itself. If I could double the landing page conversion or the email-to-call rate, the maths works. Which brings us back to the part I cannot ship.

---

**Q6:** "Have you ever hired anyone, designer, agency, freelancer, to help with the funnel assets? What happened?"

**A:** Three times, three quite different outcomes, and the pattern across all of them is the interesting part.

First, the Lisbon freelancer. Found her through a referral. She does book layouts and PDF assets, charges roughly €60 an hour, work is good. The relationship works, but the bandwidth does not. She has other clients, works in batches, goes on holiday. Fine for "I need one PDF every six months." Not fine for "I want to test four angles this quarter." The bottleneck is not her quality, it is her calendar.

Second, a German marketing agency in Hamburg. Free consultation last summer, they pitched a full funnel rebuild for €8,500 plus €2,000/month retainer. Strategy, creative, ads management, everything. The pitch deck was beautiful. But when I pushed on what I would actually get in month one, the answer was "a brand audit and a creative direction document," and in month two, "two new lead magnets and a landing page redesign." So €10,500 of spend before I had anything testable. I did not do it. Honestly the reason was not the money. It was that I could not see how I would know whether their work was good without already having done it myself once. And by then I had already done it myself once.

Third, a cheaper experiment in October, a Fiverr designer for a one-off PDF refresh. €280 for a full redesign. The file she sent back was technically clean. But the design choices made no sense for my brand, the typography pairings were generic, and revisions were €80 each beyond the first round. We did two more rounds, I spent €440 total, and I ended up using maybe 30 percent of what she made and rebuilding the rest with the Lisbon freelancer.

The pattern across all three: the work itself is not the problem, the loop is. Every time I bring a person in, I am introducing a calendar dependency and a translation cost. I have to explain my brand, I have to wait for their cycle, I have to review and feedback. The thing I actually want to buy is not "someone who can design a PDF." It is "the ability to test a new angle next Tuesday without a person being involved."

---

**Q7:** "Where does your monthly marketing spend split between time and money, and what feels worth it?"

**A:** Let me actually do the numbers because I think about this a lot.

Money side, monthly: €600 LinkedIn Ads, €99 email tool, €30 Carrd, €15 Calendly, €25 Zapier, €17 Otter, €20 Notion. Around €806/month, plus another €200 amortised across the freelancer work. Roughly €1,000/month in cash.

Time side: 13 hours a week, so 56 hours a month. If I value my own hour at the coaching rate of €350, that is €19,600/month of time going into marketing, which is a ridiculous number but the right one to look at because every hour I spend on marketing is an hour I am not delivering or selling.

So the cash spend is 5 percent of total marketing investment and time is 95 percent. That is the real picture.

What feels worth it: LinkedIn content, definitely. Even when a specific post does not perform, the cumulative effect over four years is real. I have inbound enquiries every week from people saying "I have been reading your stuff for two years, I think I am ready." That compounds.

What does not feel worth it: the funnel work. Not because it is not valuable, but because the time I spend on production rather than thinking is so disproportionate. I am spending 30 hours a quarter on lead magnet design and only 3 hours on lead magnet strategy. That is broken.

What I genuinely cannot tell: whether the ads are worth it. The unit economics are tight. If the lead magnet were better, the answer would clearly be yes. If the lead magnet stays as it is, the answer is "barely." So the ad spend is hostage to a decision I have not been able to make, which is whether to invest in upgrading the lead magnet or to test new angles.

---

**Q8:** "If the design and landing-page work just disappeared, and you only had to write the copy, what would change?"

**A:** That is a question I have asked myself almost as a fantasy, so let me try to answer it seriously.

The first thing that would change is the number of angles I could test. Today I test, generously, two angles a year. With production friction removed, my limit becomes my own creative output, which is much higher. I have a list in Notion of 11 different angles I want to try. If I could ship one a month I would burn through that list by next spring and I would actually know which angles work for which segments.

The second thing is what I would do with my time. Right now I am the bottleneck for production. If production were not a thing, I would be the bottleneck for thinking, which is a much better problem to have. More client research, more interviewing, better hypotheses.

The third thing is the ads spend. If I could iterate the lead magnet weekly, I would pour more money into ads, because I would have a flywheel. Today I cannot justify going from €600 to €1,500 a month because I cannot improve what the ads point to fast enough. The constraint on ad spend is not money. It is the asset behind the ad.

The fourth, more emotional: I would stop dreading the funnel work. There is this background guilt every week, where I know I should be testing the deal-cycle angle and instead I am moving an icon two pixels.

What would not change: the LinkedIn content. That is creative work, my voice. Same with strategy, same with client work. The thing that would disappear is the production layer between "I have a tested idea" and "the world can see and respond to it."

If you took that layer away, my revenue could realistically double in 12 months, because the lever is not "more leads at the current conversion rate," it is "better lead magnets driving better conversion." And the only reason that lever is not pulled is the production cost.

---

**Q9:** "Closing question. What is the part of your funnel that, if you could just see what was actually happening, would change the most decisions you make?"

**A:** I am going to interpret your question quite literally because there is a precise answer.

The part of the funnel I have least visibility on is the email sequence. I know my open rates because the email tool reports them. I know my click rates. But I do not know which specific paragraphs of which specific emails are causing people to either book a call or unsubscribe. I have hypotheses, but I have no data. And the email sequence is where 80 percent of the conversion work actually happens, between the PDF download and the Calendly booking.

If I could see, for example, "people who read past paragraph three of email four are six times more likely to book a call than people who do not," that would change what I write in email four. Right now I write email four based on intuition and on what worked broadly in my last cohort. That is a guess dressed up as a strategy.

Second answer: the part of the funnel where I most feel like I am flying blind is the relationship between which LinkedIn Ad creative someone clicked on and how they then behaved through the email sequence. LinkedIn tracks what happens up to the lead capture, my email tool tracks what happens after, but the join is broken. I cannot tell whether the people who came in through the "framework" angle ad behave differently downstream than people who came in through the "case study" angle. They probably do. But I cannot prove it.

Third, and this is the one I have been thinking about most: I cannot see, before I commit to producing a lead magnet, whether the angle is going to work. I can pre-test the headline on LinkedIn, which is good, but the leap from "this hook gets engagement" to "this hook turns into a 12-page asset that converts" is huge, and I make it on faith every time. If I had even a directional signal, "this angle, in this format, with this audience, is likely to outperform your baseline by 15 percent," that alone would unlock decisions I currently postpone for months because the production cost of being wrong is too high.

So three layers, in order of pain: the email sequence is opaque, cross-platform attribution is broken, and pre-production confidence does not exist. If I had to pick one to fix tomorrow, it would be the third, because it is the one currently stopping me from testing at all. The other two I can live with as a slightly more sophisticated marketer. The third is the one making my business smaller than it should be.

## 3. What This Confirmed

Three patterns from the previous interviews showed up in Tobias's conversation, and one important boundary became clearer.

**The agency PDF problem, again.** Fatema's bakery friend got a brand book and two ads for €2,000 with no clarity on what worked. Lisa got a brand PDF and three creatives for €600 with no clarity on what worked. Tobias got pitched €10,500 of "audit and creative direction" before any testable asset would exist, and walked away for the same reason: he could not see how he would know whether the work was good without having already done it himself. This is the third independent surfacing of the same failure mode. The agency model does not give SMB and solo operators the visibility they need to learn from the spend. This is not a Fatema-specific or DTC-specific pattern. It travels.

**The knowledge-gap-not-money-gap, again.** Lisa said she would run ads again if she had visibility and control. Marc said he could spend 3x more on creative if he could see which assets actually moved the needle. Anja explicitly said the constraint was not budget but iteration speed. Tobias said it almost word for word in Q7: "the constraint on ad spend is not money. It is the asset behind the ad." Four out of five interviews have produced this exact framing, unprompted. This is the strongest pattern in the discovery set. Still's wedge is not "make ads cheaper." It is "remove the production friction that stops people from spending more confidently."

**The lead-magnet bottleneck and the iteration-loop pain.** This is the question I built the interview to answer, and the answer is yes, with caveats. Tobias's experience in Q3 and Q4 is structurally identical to Marc's ad-creative iteration loop and Anja's "30 minutes of thinking, three weeks of production" complaint. The Sunday afternoon of writing that becomes 14 weeks of asset production. The 5 percent creative thinking, 95 percent production friction ratio. The calendar dependency on freelancers. The "by the time I launch, my insight is five months old" frustration. These are the same beats. The surface is different, a 12-page PDF instead of a square ad image, but the shape of the bottleneck is the same: a person with a clear hypothesis is blocked by the cost of turning that hypothesis into a testable asset.

**The boundary test, and the strategic call.** The shape of the pain is the same, but the surface is different in ways that matter for product. Still, as currently described, generates ad creative, meaning visual assets at ad-unit dimensions. A 12-page lead-magnet PDF is a different artefact, with typography hierarchy, multi-page layout, brand consistency across pages. The technology that makes a great Meta ad is not necessarily the technology that makes a great enterprise PDF. Tobias's landing page and 7-email sequence are two more surfaces beyond the PDF itself.

So the strategic question for Still: do we serve service businesses by extending into long-form lead-magnet generation and email sequence drafting, or hold the line at "performance creative for paid ads" and accept consultants like Tobias as adjacent rather than primary? My read is hold the line for now. Tobias validates the underlying thesis, the iteration-loop pain is real and not segment-specific, but he is not the right first customer because his asset surface is too varied. Marc and Anja remain the better wedge: a single asset type, ad creative, that recurs frequently, where Still's value is clearest. Tobias becomes a v2 conversation when the long-form asset generation problem is solvable.

One additional signal: Tobias is the most AI-fluent of the five interviewees by some distance. He has integrated Claude, Otter, Calendly, Zapier and his email tool into a working system. His bar for "is this AI tool actually useful" is high. He will not be wowed by a demo. He will be convinced by a measurable improvement in his iteration cycle, ideally one he can verify in an afternoon.

**From training knowledge, no single citable source:**

- **Consultant program pricing in DACH (€2,000 to €3,500 for a 6-week B2B online program):** consistent with the broad range visible in publicly listed programmes from German-language sales-training providers. €2,400 chosen as a defensible mid-market price point.
- **Lead-magnet conversion benchmarks (landing page conversion 30 to 45 percent for B2B lead-gen forms with cold traffic; email open rates declining from 50 to 60 percent on email one to 20 to 30 percent by email seven):** consistent with the email-marketing community's published benchmarks but they vary widely by audience and tool.

- **The "PDF deliverable" failure mode where an agency or freelancer produces a polished asset but the client cannot tell whether it works:** directly described by Fatema, Lisa and now Tobias, three independent observations across three different business types. That convergence is the actual evidence here, not a published study.

**Patterns drawn from previous interviews in this discovery set:**

- The iteration-loop framing in Q3 and Q4 is built explicitly to test whether the pain Marc and Anja described in the SaaS context shows up in a different surface. The "5 percent creative, 95 percent production" framing is a paraphrase of Anja's "30 minutes of thinking, three weeks of production."

- The Q9 closing question was designed to test whether the "knowledge gap, not money gap" pattern from Lisa and Anja held for Tobias. It did, and added a third layer (pre-production confidence) that the previous interviews did not surface.

**Methodology footer.** This is a synthetic interview. Tobias Ahrens is a constructed persona, not a real person. The transcript was generated through a dual-agent process, an interviewer agent posing Mom Test compliant questions and a persona agent answering in character, both grounded in the cited research above and the patterns surfaced in the Fatema, Lisa, Marc and Anja interviews. The goal of this sixth interview was to pressure-test the boundary of Still's beachhead by introducing a customer profile (solo service business with a lead-magnet funnel rather than an ad-creative funnel) at the edge of the wedge. The interview is disclosed as synthetic in the hand-in and is not represented as primary user research. It is supplementary pattern-validation, not a substitute for the real conversations with Fatema and any subsequent live interviews.
