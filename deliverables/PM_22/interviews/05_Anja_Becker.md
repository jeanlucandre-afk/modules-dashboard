# PM_22, Synthetic Customer Interview 05
## Anja Becker, Marketing Manager, B2B SaaS (TasteWise)

> This is the fifth interview in the PM_22 set and the third synthetic one. Real interviews with Fatema (SMB owner) and a constrained-team founder are already on file. To pressure-test the patterns further up the company-size curve, I built a research-backed B2B SaaS marketing manager persona, then ran a structured Mom Test interview against her. The goal was to check whether the agency-PDF and designer-bottleneck patterns survive in a context where the buyer has more budget but less unilateral authority. Disclosed as synthetic. Cited sources at the end.

---

## 1. Persona Research, Who I Built

The persona was constructed from three layers: the OpenView 2023 SaaS Benchmarks Report (n=710) for spend ratios and CAC payback bands, The B2B House / HockeyStack 2024–25 LinkedIn Ads benchmarks for CPL ranges, and the patterns Fatema and Lisa surfaced about agency relationships and creative bottlenecks. The goal was to land on a profile that is statistically average for her cohort, so anything she says reads as the median experience, not an outlier.

**Name:** Anja Becker
**Age:** 29
**Role:** Marketing Manager at TasteWise, a B2B SaaS food-industry analytics platform headquartered in Berlin
**Company stage:** ~€2M ARR, post-seed, pre-Series A
**Reports to:** Markus, the COO. Markus is a finance-and-ops brain, not a marketer.
**Team:** Anja plus one content marketer (Jonas, full-time) plus a freelance designer on monthly retainer (Pia, ~€1,800/month for ~30 hours). Three people total for marketing.
**Marketing budget:** €25k/month total, roughly 15% of ARR. This sits squarely inside the OpenView 2023 benchmark for the $1–5M ARR cohort, where marketing alone typically runs 12–18% of ARR. Allocation: €8k Google Ads, €6k LinkedIn Ads, €4k content/SEO agency, €3k events and sponsorships, €4k tools and software.
**Channel performance:** LinkedIn Ads is the channel she defends most. At a SaaS mid-market CPL of $60–$120 (per HockeyStack 2024–25), €6k buys her roughly 50–100 leads a month. Trial-to-paid conversion sits around 12%, which is below the OpenView 2022 opt-in trial median of ~25%. She knows this number is bad and reads landing-page optimisation articles obsessively.
**CAC payback target:** 18 months, set by Markus. Inside the OpenView 16–24 month median band for her cohort.
**Decision authority:** partial. She can spend up to €500/month on a tool without sign-off. Anything above that goes to Markus.
**AI usage:** heavy Notion AI for briefs and meeting notes, ChatGPT for research and rewriting. Has not yet tried AI creative tools. Read about Midjourney, was curious, but Markus is sceptical of AI imagery for what he calls "trust-sensitive B2B".
**Creative bottleneck:** Pia is brilliant but capped at 30 hours a month. Anja has a notebook full of weekly experiment ideas that die because she cannot get the assets made fast enough. She has tried building creatives herself in Canva and the results, in her words, "look like Anja made them", slightly off-brand, slightly amateur, not a thing she is proud to put behind paid spend.

This persona deliberately sits at the more constrained-buyer end of the B2B SaaS spectrum. She has real budget but lives inside a CFO-style approval ceiling, which is the part of the buyer landscape most likely to tell us something useful about how an AI creative tool would actually be bought, not just used.

## 2. Interview Transcript

**Conducted by:** Jean-Luc (founder, Mango Lab / Still)
**Format:** Mom Test, past behaviour, no pitching, specific stories
**Date:** 02.05.2026
**Duration:** ~25 minutes

---

**Q1:** "Thanks for the time, Anja. Can you walk me through what your week actually looks like? Like a normal Tuesday, what does marketing-manager-mode look like for you?"

**A:** Ja, sure. Tuesdays are our heaviest internal day. I am in the office around half eight. The first thing I do is open HubSpot and Mixpanel side by side, because Markus, my COO, wants Monday's numbers in our 10am ops standup and I do not want to be the person who shows up not knowing the trial-to-paid number from the weekend. So forty minutes pulling MQL count, SQL count, LinkedIn Ads spend from the day before, CPL by campaign. I drop it into a Notion doc that Markus and the CEO both read.

After standup I am in calls with Pia, our freelance designer, or with Jonas, who runs content. Tuesday is also when I check in with the SEO agency, GrowthFluent, based in Hamburg, thirty-minute call every two weeks. Then I have what I call my "experiment block", two hours blocked off, no meetings, where I am supposed to be designing the next LinkedIn Ads test. That block gets eaten alive maybe three Tuesdays out of four. Slack happens. Sales asks for a one-pager. Something breaks in HubSpot.

Afternoons are reactive. Apollo for outbound list-building, sometimes pulling lists for our two SDRs. Lusha as backup for enrichment when Apollo's data is patchy, which is more often than I would like, naja. Editing whatever Jonas wrote that morning, because his English is great but our ICP is American food manufacturers and the tone has to be very specific.

By six I am exhausted and the experiment block has not happened. Tuesdays look productive on the surface, lots of dashboards being updated, but the actual creative thinking, the strategic stuff, gets squeezed into the edges. Genau, that is the week.

---

**Q2:** "Tell me about your current setup with the freelance designer. How does a creative request actually move from your head into a finished asset?"

**A:** Pia is wonderful. I want to start there because what I am about to describe is not her fault, it is a capacity problem. Freelance designer, thirty hours a month on retainer, €1,800. We found her through Jonas's network, she did our deck rebrand last year. Genuinely talented, very strong on B2B aesthetics.

The process. I have a Notion board called Creative Pipeline. When I have an idea, say a new LinkedIn carousel testing a different angle on our ROI calculator, I write a brief in Notion, tag Pia, and it sits in "Requested". Pia checks the board Mondays and Thursdays. If I drop a brief Tuesday afternoon, she does not see it until Thursday. She estimates the time, moves it to "In Progress", and depending on her other clients, the asset comes back four days to two weeks later. For a single LinkedIn carousel. That is the reality.

I have ideas every day. I read a Mutiny case study Saturday over coffee and think, we should test a vertical-specific landing page for bakery. Write the brief Sunday night. Monday it goes in the queue. Eight days later, Pia delivers a clean, on-brand mockup. By that point the angle that excited me on Saturday feels old, the LinkedIn algorithm has shifted, a competitor has run something similar. The asset still gets used, it still performs okay, but the gap between the spark and the launch kills probably half of what I would otherwise test.

I have tried doing it myself in Canva. €13 a month, well within my €500 ceiling. The results are okay for an internal Slack graphic but not okay for paid spend. They look like Anja made them. Font weights slightly off, spacing amateur. Pia, gently, told me last quarter she would prefer I stop using Canva for anything client-facing because it makes the brand look inconsistent. She is right. So I have ideas, I have the budget to test them, and I have a bottleneck of one designer doing thirty hours a month who can produce maybe six to eight finished asset sets in that time. That is the constraint.

---

**Q3:** "Tell me about your last LinkedIn Ads experiment. What did you test, what happened?"

**A:** The last one was three weeks ago. I have the deck from the Markus review in front of me.

We were testing two value propositions. Our standard creative leads with "TasteWise gives food manufacturers real-time pricing intelligence". Hypothesis: "pricing intelligence" is too abstract for bakery and confectionery, because those buyers think in cocoa futures, sugar contracts, specific commodity language. The variant said, in effect, "Know your cocoa cost two weeks before your competitors do". Same product, different framing.

The test ran eleven days. €1,200 on control, €1,200 on variant, same audience: marketing and procurement decision-makers at food manufacturers, 50 to 500 employees, DACH and Benelux. The control did what it always does, CPL about €88. The variant came in at €71 CPL, click-through 23% higher.

Here is the thing. Trial-to-paid from the variant cohort, after three weeks, is 9%. Control sits at 12%. The variant generated more leads, cheaper, but they convert worse. The CAC math comes out roughly the same. I cannot tell Markus we found something. I told him the test was inconclusive. He asked whether I was going to run it longer or kill it. I said I wanted another two weeks because the sample is not big enough to call. He said fine but pull the variant if conversion does not move by mid-May.

What bothers me most, and this is the bit I do not say in the deck, is that I do not know if the creative is wrong or the audience is wrong or the landing page is wrong. The variant points to a vertical-specific landing page Pia made three weeks ago. I am running a test where three variables changed at once, because I do not have the design capacity to isolate one. I am making decisions on noise.

---

**Q4:** "How did you decide what to test in the first place, and how did you report it back internally?"

**A:** Mix of gut and data. The data part: HubSpot dashboard breaks trial-to-paid by ICP segment, and bakery and confectionery was the worst performer at about 8%, versus our best segment, beverage, at around 18%. The hypothesis was, the messaging is not landing for them. The gut part was a sales call where the prospect, a German confectionery group, said "we do not really care about pricing intelligence, we care about cocoa". That sentence rattled around in my head for a week.

Reporting it back is its own thing. Markus runs marketing review every second Monday. Thirty-minute slot. I prepare a Notion doc, three sections: what we tested, what the numbers say, what I want to do next. He reads it beforehand, which is good, but it means he comes in with questions ready. Finance brain, controller before COO. His questions are always about CAC payback and contribution margin, not creative.

For this test I walked him through why a lower CPL with worse conversion is a wash, and he understood immediately, one of the things I respect about him. Then he asked a question I could not fully answer: "if we had run this test six weeks earlier, would we have made better budget allocation decisions in Q1?" Honest answer is yes. We over-invested in beverage in Q1 because the data looked good, but the data looked good partly because the messaging worked there, not because the segment is structurally better. He let it go but I could see he was filing it.

After the meeting I update a Notion page called "Experiment Log". Jonas reads it. Pia does not.

---

**Q5:** "You mentioned the SEO agency, GrowthFluent. How did you pick them, and what does the relationship actually look like in practice?"

**A:** Naja. GrowthFluent. Eight months in. €4,000 a month, real chunk of the budget. They were brought in before I joined, by Markus, on a recommendation from another portfolio company in our investor's network. Not my choice. I inherited them.

First three months were good. Content audit, twelve high-intent keywords we were not ranking for, a content calendar, four pieces of long-form content I genuinely liked. The fourth piece, on dynamic pricing for ingredient procurement, ranks number three on Google for its target keyword and drives about 200 organic visits a month. So I cannot say they have been useless.

Months four through eight have been harder. Content velocity dropped from four pieces a month to two. The pieces feel more generic, and twice in the last quarter they sent drafts I am almost certain were AI-generated, which is fine in principle, but the prompts were not specific to our ICP and the writing showed it. I asked on the last call and they said they "use AI as a research aid only", which is the answer you give when you do not want to talk about it.

The reporting is the other thing. Every month they send a PDF. Twenty-two pages. Brand colours, charts, executive summary. First three pages are organic traffic up, which, fine, organic traffic is up. The next eighteen pages are activity reports: keywords researched, briefs delivered, internal links built, technical SEO fixes recommended. Almost no pages on pipeline contribution. When I ask, they tell me SEO is a long game, which is true, and also a thing people say when they cannot show you the number you actually want.

I have started, quietly, building a case for moving the €4,000 in-house. A senior content marketer plus more design capacity, I think we would do better content for less money. Have not pitched it to Markus yet because he hired them. Contract is up in October. I will probably make my move in August.

---

**Q6:** "Where does most of your tool budget actually go, and what is getting cancelled this quarter?"

**A:** The €4,000 tools line is more crowded than people realise. Let me run it. HubSpot Marketing Hub Pro, €890, non-negotiable, our entire MQL-to-SQL flow runs through it. LinkedIn Sales Navigator three seats at about €80 each, €240. Apollo, €390 for our outbound tier. Lusha, €150, backup enrichment. Mixpanel, €210, technically owned by product but I am the heaviest user. Mutiny we trialled and dropped, €1,200 and we could not justify it at our traffic level. Intercom, €420, shared with support. Notion business plan, €120. Canva Pro €13, Loom €15, Calendly €20. Long tail of Grammarly, Otter, Zapier, maybe €180 combined.

That is about €2,650 of the €4,000. The rest is things I forget about. €110 a month for an old Hotjar account I am not sure anyone has logged into since January. A Demandbase trial that converted to paid at €450 and I need to cancel before the next renewal. A Drift account from before we moved to Intercom, somehow still being charged €180. That one is on me.

What is getting cancelled this quarter: Drift, Hotjar, Demandbase. Frees up roughly €740 a month. I want to redirect that into either a creative tool, if I can find one I trust, or more of Pia's hours. Probably Pia, because I know that ROI. The creative tool feels like a bet and Markus does not love bets.

What frustrates me about the stack is I am paying for a lot of analytics and not very much for the thing I am actually short on, which is creative output. The ratio is wrong. I can see exactly what is failing in a hundred dashboards and still not fix it because I cannot get the new asset made.

---

**Q7:** "Tell me about the last time you wanted to buy a tool that needed Markus's approval. What happened?"

**A:** This just happened. Mutiny. Personalisation tool, landing-page personalisation by industry and company size. I wanted it badly. Sales call in February, they ran a free audit on our site, identified three places where a vertical-specific variant would probably lift conversion. Pricing started at €1,200 a month for our tier.

I built the case carefully. Notion doc, three scenarios: pessimistic 5% lift on trial signups, base case 15%, optimistic 25%. Converted to incremental MRR using our 12% trial-to-paid and average ACV of €840. Pessimistic paid back in nine months, base case four, optimistic under two. Pulled three case studies from their site, one a B2B SaaS at our stage showing a 22% lift.

Sent the doc to Markus Wednesday, asked for thirty minutes Friday. Within ten minutes he reframed the whole conversation. First question: "What is your trial-to-paid right now?" 12%. He said: "OpenView median for opt-in trials is what, 25%? So we are roughly half the median. Are we sure the problem is the landing page?" I had no good answer. The 12% might be landing page, onboarding, ICP, or product. Mutiny only fixes one of those.

He did not say no. He said: "show me you can lift trial-to-paid by two points using what you already have, and I will sign off on Mutiny in Q3." Fair. And brutal. Because to lift by two points using what I already have, I need exactly the thing I cannot easily produce, more creative variants on the landing page and onboarding emails. The budget for the tool depends on me solving the problem the tool was meant to help solve.

I left that meeting respectful and frustrated in equal parts. The €500 ceiling is not really €500. It is "anything above €500 has to survive a Markus interrogation", and the interrogation is not unreasonable, just much more expensive than the tool itself.

---

**Q8:** "If Pia suddenly had unlimited capacity, what would you do differently next quarter?"

**A:** Naja, I have thought about this. There is a list.

First, run vertical-specific creative for our top four ICP segments instead of just bakery. That is roughly twelve creative variants for LinkedIn, four landing-page variants, four email sequences. Right now I can only afford one segment per quarter because that is what Pia's hours allow. With unlimited capacity I would do all four in parallel and learn something statistically meaningful within six weeks instead of six months.

Second, test creative refresh cadence. Our LinkedIn creatives run about six weeks before they fatigue, CPL drifts up 30% by week five. With unlimited capacity I would refresh every two weeks and see if the CAC math works better. I genuinely do not know if it would, that is why it would be a test.

Third, actually populate our website with the case studies customer success has been promising for nine months. Testimonials sitting in Notion from happy customers, no designed case study pages because Pia is busy. That alone would help trial-to-paid.

Fourth, the soft one, I would have time to think. Right now I do not. I am running every week just to keep existing channels alive. I read HockeyStack's CRO content, Demand Curve's playbooks, and I have a list of forty-three experiments I want to run, and I have run maybe six in the last two quarters. The bottleneck is not ideas. It is not budget, technically, the budget is there. It is creative output capacity. Genau.

If I am honest, the thing I would do is hire a second freelance designer. €1,800 a month. Markus would ask me to prove the ROI before approving, and I cannot prove the ROI because I do not have the second designer to run the experiments that would generate the proof. Same loop as Mutiny.

---

**Q9:** "Closing one. What is the question about your marketing performance that you cannot answer to Markus right now, and that bugs you?"

**A:** Oh, easy. There is a specific one. He asked me in March: "Of our €25k a month, how much is necessary and how much is just inertia?" And I could not answer it. I still cannot answer it cleanly.

The reason I cannot answer it is that I have attribution data for the channels but not for the experiments I am not running. I can tell him exactly what LinkedIn Ads cost us per lead. I cannot tell him what the creative quality of those ads is costing us, because I have never been able to test creative quality at any meaningful scale. I am defending a CPL of €88 and a trial conversion of 12% as if those are properties of the channel, when they might just be properties of the creative I happened to be able to ship that quarter. That is the question that bugs me, because it implies that maybe a meaningful chunk of the €6k LinkedIn line is paying for mediocre creative, and I have no way to prove or disprove it without more design capacity.

The second one, related, is the trial-to-paid number. 12%. Below the OpenView 25% opt-in median. I have been told this number is "the landing page", "the onboarding", "the ICP", "the product", and "the pricing" by five different people in the last six months, and the truthful answer is I do not know which. Probably some combination. But to actually figure out which, I would need to run isolated tests, and I cannot run isolated tests because every test requires creative I do not have time to make. So I keep reporting the number, every month, and Markus keeps looking at it, and we both know it is not where it should be, and neither of us has a clean theory of why.

The thing that bugs me most, underneath all of it, is that I think I am a good marketer. I read everything, I know the playbooks, I have ideas every day. And I cannot prove I am a good marketer because the constraint between my brain and the market is design capacity. If I had a tool, or a person, that closed that gap, I think I could show numbers that would change Markus's posture entirely. Right now I am defending mediocre numbers with sophisticated decks, and we both know it, and we are both too polite to say it that way out loud.

## 3. What This Confirmed

Three patterns held against the prior interviews, with interesting variations.

**The agency PDF problem, scaled up.** Lisa paid €600 to a freelancer and got a beautiful PDF and three creatives that did not work. Anja pays GrowthFluent €4,000 a month and gets a beautiful 22-page monthly PDF with eighteen pages of activity reporting and almost nothing on pipeline contribution. Same failure mode, same emotional texture ("they tell me SEO is a long game, which is a thing people say when they cannot show you the number you actually want"), different price point. The PDF problem is not a small-business problem. It scales linearly with budget. The difference is that Anja has a longer leash, a contract that runs to October, and political cover to absorb the failure for a few more months than Lisa could ever afford. The mechanism is identical.

**The designer-bottleneck, in B2B clothing.** Lisa is the designer, the founder, and the strategist, and her bottleneck is herself plus a phone. Anja has a budget, a freelancer, and a brand system, and her bottleneck is still creative output capacity. Forty-three experiments in Notion, six executed. Every channel diagnostic she has conflates creative quality with channel quality because she cannot run an isolated test without burning a week of Pia's hours. The pattern is not "small brands cannot afford designers". The pattern is "the gap between marketing ideation speed and creative production speed is structural at every company size below maybe €20M ARR". Anja's version is just better-funded.

**Constrained-buyer authority is the real story for B2B.** This is where Anja diverges most sharply from Lisa. Lisa has full buying authority and her constraint is internal confidence in the product. Anja has €500 of unilateral authority and her constraint is Markus, who is fair, technical, and absolutely will ask "what is your trial-to-paid right now?" before approving anything bigger. The Mutiny story is the cleanest evidence here: Anja built a four-month-payback case with three sourced case studies and lost not because the case was weak but because Markus reframed the underlying problem one layer up. For any AI creative tool aimed at her cohort, the buying motion is not "Anja decides". It is "Anja builds the case and survives the Markus interrogation". The product has to be cheap enough to slip under the €500 ceiling for the trial, and then good enough to produce a number Markus respects within one quarter, or it does not get bought. That is a much narrower selling window than the Lisa profile. It also means Anja is not the buyer, she is the champion. The buyer is Markus, and Markus does not care about creative output speed, he cares about CAC payback. The pitch has to translate.

The synthesis across Fatema, Lisa, and Anja is that the bottleneck is the same shape at every revenue tier. The buyer's relationship to the bottleneck changes. Fatema is constrained by team structure. Lisa is constrained by her own confidence. Anja is constrained by an approval ceiling. Same wound, different bandage.

## 4. Where the Data Came From

The persona was constructed from research-backed sources where possible, training knowledge where not, and the prior interviews for behavioural patterns. Here is the breakdown.

**Verified with real sources:**

- **B2B SaaS marketing spend benchmarks ($1–5M ARR cohort: S&M 40–50% of ARR, marketing alone 12–18%, CAC payback 16–24 months):** OpenView 2023 SaaS Benchmarks Report (n=710), https://openviewpartners.com/2023-saas-benchmarks-report/
- **Trial-to-paid conversion (median ~17%, opt-in trials median ~25%):** OpenView 2022 Product Benchmarks, https://openviewpartners.com/2022-product-benchmarks/
- **LinkedIn Ads CPL for SaaS mid-market ($60–$120):** The B2B House / HockeyStack 2024–25 LinkedIn Ad Benchmarks, https://www.theb2bhouse.com/linkedin-ad-benchmarks/

Honest caveat on the OpenView figures: the report is US-weighted. DACH B2B SaaS likely sits within ±10% of these bands but precise European-only benchmarks are harder to source. Anja's 15% marketing-of-ARR ratio, 12% trial conversion, and 18-month CAC payback target were all chosen to land inside the cited bands, so her numbers read as median-cohort, not outlier.

**From training knowledge (no single verifiable URL):**

- The €1,800/month freelance designer retainer figure for ~30 hours of B2B work in Berlin reflects common rate discussions in DACH freelance design communities. Defensible as a midpoint.
- The HubSpot, LinkedIn Sales Navigator, Apollo, Lusha, Mutiny, Mixpanel, and Intercom subscription prices Anja cites are approximate but within published-tier ranges as of 2024–25.
- The "agency PDF deliverable" failure mode at the €4k/month tier is widely documented anecdotally in B2B marketing communities (RevGenius, Demand Curve, Wynter) but no single study quantifies the pattern. The triangulation here is that two synthetic interviews and one real one (Fatema) independently surfaced the same failure mode at three different price points.

**Methodology footer:**

This interview is synthetic. Anja Becker is a constructed persona, not a real person. The interview was generated through dual-agent prompting in Claude, with the interviewer agent constrained to Mom Test rules and the persona agent grounded in the cited research plus the patterns surfaced in the Fatema (real) and Lisa (synthetic) interviews. The goal was to test whether the agency-PDF and creative-bottleneck patterns survive at a B2B SaaS company size where the buyer has real budget but constrained authority. They survived, with the additional finding that the buying motion at Anja's tier is mediated by a finance-brain approver whose questions reframe the problem one layer above the tool being purchased. That finding has direct implications for how an AI creative product would be positioned and priced for this cohort.
