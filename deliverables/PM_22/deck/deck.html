KA1 · 01 of 03
Problem statement.
Page 04 of 52

Five out of six SMBs we interviewed described the same broken transaction with their marketing agencies, without us asking.

The project this deck is built around is Mango Lab Still, a software product I am building with my co-founder Alberto. Still helps small and medium businesses run paid social ads on Meta, LinkedIn and Google without hiring a marketing agency. We have two paying clients so far: meetDWIGHT, a B2B SaaS app, and a small marketing agency that resells our work.

Before writing a single line of product code, we ran six discovery interviews. One was a real conversation with Fatema, a marketing coordinator at CODE University. The other five are synthetic personas built using Claude with cited research, fully labelled and detailed in the appendix.

In five of the six, the same story came up unprompted. A small business owner pays an agency or freelancer between €600 and €4,000 for a campaign. A polished PDF arrives. A handful of static ads go live. The Meta dashboard shows clicks but not why. The campaign ends and nobody can explain what worked. Money exchanged, clarity not.

That ad creation was the binding constraint for SMBs running paid social. That faster creative production was the wedge. That the customer was the in-house marketer doing the work themselves.

The binding constraint is not creative production speed. It is creative iteration visibility. The customer is not always the marketer doing the work. Sometimes it is the founder watching the work get done and not understanding any of it. Different problem. Different product.

My twist on the journey map

Standard maps weight stages equally. Honest weighting: stage 03 ≈ 60%, stage 05 ≈ 30%, decisions ≈ 5%. The product should match those ratios, not the textbook.

KA3 · 01 of 04
Three techniques. Eight candidates. Two survived.
Page 16 of 52

I used three creativity techniques in sequence. Each one filtered the survivors of the previous round.

How-Might-We. This is the standard divergent-ideation move from design thinking: take a pain point and rephrase it as an open question that invites many answers. I started from the user journey map and asked, "How might we compress the two-week creative iteration loop without sacrificing brand voice fidelity?" That single question generated 14 candidate framings of the product. I dropped 9 of them as obvious dead ends, kept 5 to test further.

SCAMPER on the survivors. SCAMPER is a checklist by Bob Eberle (Substitute / Combine / Adapt / Modify / Put-to-other-use / Eliminate / Reverse) used to push an existing idea harder. The most useful move on our 5 survivors was Adapt: what do learning apps like Brilliant.org or Duolingo do that an ad creative tool could borrow? The answer was spaced repetition for creative variants. It did not survive my validation step, but the detour clarified what we were actually competing against.

Idiometrics — the Business Killers move. Idiometrics is the framework taught in our PM_22 LU sessions for stress-testing product ideas. Its Business Killers move inverts the standard ideation question. Instead of asking "what could this product do?", it asks "what would kill this product before it ever finds product-market fit?" I listed 12 candidate killers. The three that mattered were generic brand voice, opaque attribution, and slow iteration. Those three killers became the three core feature requirements for Mango Lab Still.

My twist on creativity sequencing

Standard ideation generates options. Idiometrics-style killer-listing inverts that and starts from failure. Running it last let me discard things faster than running it first would have, but in retrospect I would run it first next time. It is faster than divergent ideation, and it forces honesty about what the product is actually competing against.

One thing this product is not.

01

02

The thesis. SMBs do not need faster ad creative. They need visible iteration. The bottleneck is not production. It is the loop between trying, learning, and trying again. Solve the loop and the production speed follows.

What this product is not. Not another AI image generator. Not another ad-copy generator. Not a Canva alternative. Mango Lab Still is the iteration layer that sits on top of those tools. The image and the copy are commodities. The loop is the moat.

Hypothesis we killed in ideation

The first version of the product was "AI generates 50 ad variants per brief." We killed it after the Pencil interview pattern. Generating more variants is not the bottleneck. Knowing which variant to ship is. The 50-variants version is also why every existing AI ad tool fails.

KA4 · 01 of 04
Prototype: Wizard-of-Oz, then Engine_001.
Page 20 of 52

I ran two prototypes back to back. Each one tested a different question. Both were sold to real paying clients.

Prototype 01: Wizard-of-Oz with meetDWIGHT, €500. A "wizard-of-Oz" prototype is one where the customer experiences a finished product but the work happens manually behind the scenes. There was no Mango Lab Still platform yet. I generated the creative myself using Claude, Midjourney and Figma, then delivered it to the client (meetDWIGHT, a B2B SaaS app) as if it had come out of a product. The question I was testing: will an SMB pay real money for an AI-driven ad creative service before any technology exists to back it? The answer was yes — the invoice was paid, and the client came back for a second engagement.

Prototype 02: Engine_001, a €400 Meta Ads sprint. Run on a real client account (the small marketing agency that resells our work) over fourteen days. Five creative variants generated through our voice-corpus method, with a live attribution dashboard built in Notion that linked each variant to its qualified leads. The question this prototype tested: does running five creative variants per week beat one variant per week on cost per qualified lead? That is the iteration-cadence hypothesis.

Both prototypes share a deliberate constraint. Neither one is autonomous. I am personally in the loop end-to-end. The point of these prototypes was never "does the AI work?" The point was "does the customer pay for this workflow when they experience it?"

Why I ran two prototypes, not one

The Wizard-of-Oz tested willingness-to-pay (a year-shaped question). Engine_001 tested iteration cadence (a month-shaped question). Each prototype isolated one variable. If I had run them together I would have learned nothing — any signal would have been confounded.

The Still v0 scope is three features. Two paying clients are already lined up to test it. Everything else is deliberately out.

An MVP (Minimum Viable Product) in Lean Startup terms is the smallest possible version of a product that still tests the core hypothesis. For Mango Lab Still, the core hypothesis is that a brand-trained AI can compress the creative iteration loop from two weeks to two days without losing voice quality. The smallest thing that tests that is three features, no more.

What is in Still v0. First, voice corpus ingest: a customer drops a link to their Notion or Google Drive folder, and the system reads it to learn the brand's tone. Second, variant generation: given a campaign brief, the system produces five creative variants ranked by how closely they match the corpus. Third, a live attribution dashboard that pulls Meta and Google Ads conversion events and links each variant to its qualified leads.

What is deliberately out. No multi-user accounts. No single sign-on. No automated Stripe billing (invoices are manual for now). No mobile. No public API. No white-label version. No "autonomous AI agent" mode. Each of these would be useful eventually, but none of them tests the core hypothesis. They belong on the post-product-market-fit roadmap, not in v0.

Acceptance criterion. v0 is finished when meetDWIGHT and the agency client can run a full Engine_001-style sprint inside the product without my involvement. Target ship date: 2026-07-15. Alberto, my technical co-founder, owns the build.

My twist on MVP scoping

Most teams scope an MVP additively, listing what they want to include. The honest move is subtractive. The v0 scope above is what survived after I cut 14 features I personally wanted in. Each cut was tested against one question: "does this feature directly serve H-01 (voice fidelity) or H-02 (iteration cadence)?" If the answer was no, the feature was cut.

My twist on Build-Measure-Learn

The textbook Lean Startup treats the loop as a single repeating unit. In practice, a loop has a natural size set by the bet inside it. Big bets need long loops. Small bets need short loops. Mismatch the two and you either waste budget or learn nothing.

Trend-scenario analysis, three 2030s.

Three plausible futures for AI ad creative. Mango Lab needs to survive at least two.

Trend-scenario analysis is the strategic-foresight technique of imagining three different futures based on how today's trends could play out, then asking which strategic moves work in more than one. Below are the three I take seriously for Mango Lab Still.

Scenario A · The native default
Foundation models ship native ad tooling.

By 2027 OpenAI, Anthropic and Google all expose first-class APIs that take a brand kit and a campaign brief and produce ad creative directly. The voice-fidelity problem is solved at the model layer, no longer at the application layer.

What Mango Lab does: shifts from creative generation to attribution + iteration, the parts those APIs do not solve. The dashboard is the moat, not the model.

Scenario B · The compliance squeeze
EU AI Act enforcement bites hard.

By 2028 Article 50 has been enforced with at least one fine over €5M against a major SaaS player for unmarked AI ad output. The regulation creates real friction for both SMBs and the foundation-model giants.

What Mango Lab does: compliance becomes a feature. Watermarking, lineage, audit trails ship by default. Mango Lab moves from "nice to have" to "regulatorily required."

Scenario C · The agency comeback
SMBs trust humans again.

By 2029 the AI hype cycle has burned enough SMBs that they pull back to human agencies for premium work, even at a price premium. AI tools become a commodity layer underneath the agency's services.

What Mango Lab does: doubles down on Sven (agency owner) as the primary ICP. Mango Lab becomes the back-end creative tool agencies licence per-seat, not the consumer-facing brand.

The strategic question is not which scenario will happen. It is which of our investments work in more than one. Voice-corpus IP works in A and B. Attribution dashboard works in all three. The Sven-as-channel hypothesis works strongly in C and weakly in A. Building the dashboard first is the move that survives all three futures. That is why the MoSCoW from the previous section ranks attribution as a Must-Have.

My twist on scenario analysis

The textbook move is to score scenarios on probability and pick the most likely. The honest move is to score your investments on cross-scenario robustness and pick the ones that survive in two or three. Robustness beats prediction.

The frameworks taught in PM_22 are a toolkit, not a doctrine. Three worked. One did not. One needed serious adaptation.

Earned its place
Idiometrics Business Killers.

The single highest-leverage technique I picked up this semester. Inverting "what could the product do" into "what would kill the product" cut the candidate feature set from 14 to 3 in one afternoon. I will use this on every product I work on from now on.

Earned its place
Mom Test interview format.

Rob Fitzpatrick's discipline of asking past-behaviour questions instead of hypothetical ones held up across all six interviews. The agency-PDF pattern only surfaced because no one in the conversation pitched anything. "What did you do last time" beats "would you buy this" every time.

Earned its place
DUFVS scored honestly.

The five-dimension stack from Disciplined Entrepreneurship is rigorous when you actually use the yellow score. I left Sustainability at 5/10 because I do not have measured kWh data. Faking green there would have been the easy move and the wrong one.

Value Proposition Canvas.

Strategyzer's VPC works for products with a settled Job-to-be-Done. Did not earn its place
SCAMPER as ideation engine.

SCAMPER is great for refining a known idea. It is not great for generating one from scratch. The two hours I spent on Substitute / Combine / Adapt produced nothing that survived to the killer-list, and the detour cost time the Idiometrics step would have saved. Use SCAMPER on a survivor, not on a candidate.

Did not earn its place
Standard Kano (without time).

Kano's Must / Performance / Delight categories are useful, but the framework treats them as static. In practice, today's delighter is tomorrow's must-have, and Kano on its own does not show that drift. I added a temporal axis (where each feature sits in 2026 vs 2028) and that made it useful.

What this timeline shows that the linear deck does not: the killing of H-03 (the volume thesis) happened before Engine_001 even launched, in mid-March. KA3 ideation work continued throughout April even though the deck presents it as a discrete section. The persona research for the synthetic interviews happened in late April, after most of the validation work was already done. The KAs were a way of organising the writing, not the work.

The most expensive lesson in the timeline is that Engine_001 should have launched two weeks earlier. The two weeks of additional discovery between mid-March and the Engine_001 start did not produce new insights; they produced confidence I could have gained more cheaply by running the sprint sooner.

My twist on showing the timeline

Most product decks hide the messiness because it looks unprofessional. Showing it explicitly sends a different signal: the writer knows the difference between how work is presented and how it actually happens. That is the signal worth sending.
