## BEAT 2. What Morozov actually argues (0:40 – 3:00)

In 2013, Evgeny Morozov published a book called *To Save Everything, Click Here: The Folly of Technological Solutionism*. The book has one big idea and one supporting idea, and it is worth getting both of them right before I touch them.

The big idea is solutionism. Morozov borrows the word from architecture and urban planning, and he is open about the fact that he means it as an insult. He defines it as:

`[QUOTE CARD: "an unhealthy preoccupation with sexy, monumental, and narrow-minded solutions, the kind of stuff that wows audiences at TED Conferences, to problems that are extremely complex, fluid, and contentious." (Morozov, p. 5)]`

The move he keeps making, again and again, is this. A solutionist looks at a messy human situation, smoking, voting, cooking, recycling, and reframes it as a problem with a clean answer. Once it is a problem with a clean answer, the only interesting question left is which gadget to build. Morozov quotes the design theorist Michael Dobbins, who says solutionism reaches "for the answer before the questions have been fully asked." That is the whole critique in one line.

The supporting idea is what Morozov calls internet-centrism. This is the assumption that "the Internet" is a coherent thing with a coherent logic, and that its logic should be imported into other domains. Politics should work like Wikipedia. Journalism should work like Twitter. Civic life should work like a Groupon coupon. He spends most of chapter two pulling that assumption apart and showing how empty it is once you stop using it as a slogan.

`[VISUAL: cut to two of his examples.]`

His favourite illustrations are small and revealing. There is BinCam, a smart trash bin that takes a photo of your rubbish and posts it to Facebook so your friends can score your recycling. There is a teapot that glows red when the national grid is overloaded. There is the gamification of voting, of dieting, of grief. Each one looks innocent. Each one, Morozov argues, is doing the same thing: taking a moral or civic act, the kind of act that used to involve duty, judgement, friction, and replacing it with a tracked metric and a reward.

His punchline, on page fifteen of the introduction, is a sentence I keep going back to:

`[QUOTE CARD: "Imperfection, ambiguity, opacity, disorder, and the opportunity to err, to sin, to do the wrong thing: all of these are constitutive of human freedom." (Morozov, p. xv)]`

That is the book in one breath. A world without friction is also a world without us.

## BEAT 3. Why this is not a dead critique (3:00 – 4:30)

The book is from 2013. Most of his examples are from a moment that already feels quaint. Smart bins. Glowing teapots. Foursquare check-ins. So a fair question is whether any of this still bites in 2026.

`[VISUAL: three real product landing pages, taglines highlighted.]`

I went looking for the answer the way Morozov would. Not in his archive, in the present tense. I pulled three taglines from current AI tool homepages. The first one says "creativity, solved." The second one says "we removed the boring parts of work." The third one says "your team, but ten times faster." None of those sentences would have been out of place in his 2013 footnotes. The only thing that has changed is the engine under the hood. The reframe is the same. A complex human activity, creativity, work, collaboration, has been recast as a tractable problem with a paid solution.

In 2023, Marc Andreessen published *The Techno-Optimist Manifesto*, which is the most honest restatement of solutionism I have read. He writes:

`[QUOTE CARD: "We believe that there is no material problem, whether created by nature or by technology, that cannot be solved with more technology." (Andreessen, 2023)]`

And later:

`[QUOTE CARD: "Give us a real world problem, and we can invent technology that will solve it." (Andreessen, 2023)]`

Morozov's 2013 book reads like a direct response to a manifesto written ten years after it. That is not a dead critique. That is a critique that has aged into the room.

## BEAT 4. Where Morozov is right, applied to my own field (4:30 – 6:15)

I want to apply the critique to the thing I actually build. AI ad creative for small businesses. The category pitch, in every deck, is some version of: making ads is slow, agencies are expensive, the blank page is paralysing, we fix all three.

If I run that pitch through Morozov, three things fall out.

One. "Slow" is not a real problem for most of the small businesses I talk to. The agency took three weeks because the strategy conversation took three weeks. The generation step, the part my tool replaces, was always the cheap part. Compressing it from days to seconds does not give them a better ad. It gives them a faster blank page.

Two. "Expensive" is real, and this is the place where the tool earns its keep. A bakery in Hamburg cannot pay an agency two thousand euros for a campaign. She can pay forty euros a month for software. Lowering that floor is not solutionism. That is access.

Three. "Blank page" is the slipperiest one, because it sounds like a problem and it is actually a symptom. The reason a small business owner stares at a blank page is not that she lacks a generator. It is that nobody has helped her decide what she wants to say. A generator that produces twelve variants of a non-decision is not a solution. It is twelve coats of paint on a missing wall.

So Morozov is right where it counts. The category, including my company, is selling the speed of the easy part as if it were the hard part. That is solutionism. I am not above this. I have written that pitch deck.

---

## BEAT 5. Where Morozov overreaches (6:15 – 8:00)

The honest part of this video is the next ninety seconds, because Morozov is also wrong in places, and the essay does not work if I will not say so.

His tone flattens everything. By the end of the book, a smart trash bin and a predictive policing system are both filed under "solutionism," and the word starts to do too much work. There is a real difference between an app that nudges me to drink water and an algorithm that decides whether I get bail. Treating both as the same kind of mistake makes the critique easier to write and easier to dismiss.

He also underweights agency. The implicit reader of his book is a passive consumer of Silicon Valley products. The actual builder, the actual user, the actual regulator, all of them can read Morozov and design against him. I did. That option is missing from his account.

And he is stronger on the diagnosis than on what to do instead. The closing chapters call for "imperfection by design," which sounds beautiful and tells me almost nothing about how to ship a Tuesday release.

This is where I want to bring in the Californian Ideology, because Barbrook and Cameron, writing in 1995, name the worldview Morozov is critiquing better than Morozov does himself.

`[QUOTE CARD: "a bizarre fusion of the cultural bohemianism of San Francisco with the hi-tech industries of Silicon Valley... in the digital utopia, everybody will be both hip and rich." (Barbrook & Cameron, 1995)]`

Solutionism did not fall from the sky in 2013. It is the operating system of a much older worldview, eighteen years older at least, and that worldview survives every individual product Morozov dismantles. So the fight is not really with the bin. It is with the ideology that keeps producing more bins.

`[VISUAL: a one-line nod to Zuboff: "Once a problem is reframed, the next move is to harvest it." (Zuboff, *Surveillance Capitalism*, 2019)]`

---

## BEAT 6. My own position (8:00 – 9:30)

So what do I keep, after building a thing Morozov would put on his cover?

One sentence. Before you ask whether a tool is good, ask whose problem this was.

`[VISUAL: hand-drawn whiteboard. The sentence written out. Then a second line underneath.]`

We had a feature on the Mango Lab roadmap called "tone of voice generator." On paper it was the obvious next thing. Click a button, get five tone variants, pick one. We killed it last month. The reason is the reason of this video. A small business does not have a tone of voice problem. She has a strategy problem, a "who am I talking to and why" problem, and dressing that up as a generation problem would have been the exact move Morozov is warning about. We replaced the feature with a fifteen minute conversation. It is slower. It is also the actual work.

That is what I mean by keeping Morozov honestly. Not agreeing with him about everything, and not dismissing him because some of it is overcooked. Using him as a question I run my own roadmap through. Whose problem was this. If the answer is "nobody's, we invented it," do not ship.

`[VISUAL: cut back to JL on camera, no graphics.]`

---

## BEAT 7. Close (9:30 – 10:00)

Morozov is half right on purpose. The other half is the work, and the work happens inside the companies he is suspicious of, by people who have read him and decided to keep building anyway. The question I want to leave with is not whether your tool is neutral. That one is settled. The question is which of your defaults you would be willing to defend in public, by name, on camera, the way I am trying to right now.

`[VISUAL: hold on a still frame. Final card.]`
`[CARD: "Technology is neither good nor bad; nor is it neutral." (Kranzberg, 1986)]`

Fade.
