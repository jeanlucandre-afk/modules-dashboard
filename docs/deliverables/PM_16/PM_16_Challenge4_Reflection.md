Who did you reach out to, and why did you admire them?

I started by making a proper list. Thirty-two people across different regions, different stages, different industries but all connected in some way to building products, growing brands, or figuring out how to sell. Some were in the DTC and performance marketing world directly because that is the space Mango Lab lives in. Some were founders I had been following for years just because I found them interesting. A few were from Costa Rica or Latin America, which mattered to me personally because I do not see that world represented much in the Berlin startup bubble.

For 20 of them I used a GPT prompt to generate a personalised first message. I fed it their profile, a recent post, and a specific question I actually wanted answered. For the other 12 I wrote the messages manually. I got a few replies. Most of them said something like "I would love to but things are crazy right now, circle back in a month." A few did not reply at all. In the end the conversation that actually happened was with my dad. He has been running his own business my whole life and the whole time I was building Mango Lab I had not really sat down and asked him how he thinks about selling. So I did. That ended up being the best conversation I had.


What was the most valuable or surprising piece of advice you received during your chat? (Share a specific story or direct quote).

I kept waiting for something more complicated and instead he basically said: "the first person who pays you is not buying the product. They are buying you. They are betting on you as a person." He told me about the early years of his business, when he had no brand and no track record and the only thing he could put on the table was himself. He would show up early, remember details about the client, call back the same day. Not because he had a system but because he genuinely did not want to let people down. He said at some point you stop needing to pitch because the people around you already know how you work and they refer you. That stayed with me. We had been so focused on the product and the acquisition side and the messaging, and my dad described the first sale as a personal bet, not a product decision.


Reflect on the outreach process. What did you learn about crafting a message that gets a reply from a busy, successful person?

The automation taught me something. For the 20 messages I ran through GPT, I fed it the profile, a recent post, and the specific question I wanted to ask. The output was actually decent. Most of those messages read as personalised because the inputs were personalised. But there is a ceiling. The ones that went nowhere were the ones where the question was still generic even after the personalisation, something I had reverse-engineered from their profile rather than something I had actually thought about. The manual messages were slower but the ones that got replies were the ones where I had a genuinely specific reason to reach out. The learning is that personalisation is the floor. You can automate the floor. You cannot automate the part where you actually have something interesting to say. And the best conversation I had came from the person closest to me, which tells me I had been underestimating who was already in my network.
