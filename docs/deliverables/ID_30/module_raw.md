Module Description
Artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) techniques are rapidly entering mainstream use in a wide variety of digital product contexts. This module explores AI and ML as design materials, investigating the potentials and challenges of AI techniques in an interaction design and creative context. Indicative areas of study in this module include: An introduction to the current AI arena and machine learning environment, including techniques and domains such as Speech to Text (STT), Text to Speech (TTS), Computer Vision (CV), Machine Learning (ML) applications of neural network types, Large Language Models, and applications and processes of large data sets. The course also considers issues associated with AI and machine learning regulatory frameworks and ethics. Additional indicative study areas include the creation and use of datasets (e.g., expert systems, recommender systems, computer vision, and machine learning), practical applications of ML and AI techniques approached from a design context, and the use of high-level tools in prototype scenarios and discussion of the creative and design opportunities and challenges of working with smart tools and processes.\n\nThis module is taught as a Studio Module, with mandatory participation every week, exploring principles, ideas and design techniques and using simple code and design prototyping examples exploring different topics. There will be reading tasks and examples set as preparation for each session that are expected to be completed in advance, week by week. We will use elements of the coding environment p5.js / Processing so a basic understanding of coding is expected.

Policy on the usage of AI tools
You can decide to use genAI tools or not in this assignment.
You MUST put your decision clearly on the front page of your annotated portfolio with a short reflection on the reasons for your decision.
If you choose to use GenAI tools you must include details of the prompts, revisions and some overview of how you used the tools in your portfolio. Please ensure that your tools are GDPR compliant.
One of the objectives of the AI in Design  course is to teach how to use a range of AI tools and techniques in a responsible manner. This means that you are allowed to use generative AI as a tool for exploration, in a responsible manner. There are a number of AI techniques for you to use and explore with, and you are free to use other generative AI tools as well. Still, there are limits to what we allow: we are grading your performance and development in the course, not the performance of the LLM or any generative AI tool. This means that we always want to see your agency, and your decisions. You can generate images, texts, code, models or other elements, as long as you provide proper attribution and clear reference to the material that was generated by AI. Your final deliverable, that is the annotated portfolio  can (must) contain your generated materials, but need to be assembled and annotated by you as group/individual. It is explicitly not allowed to generate the complete deliverables, not in this course and not in any course. Please be very mindful of adding academic references, LLMs are known to hallucinate, and any fabricated references will be always reported to the EO as fraud.
A note on data and genAI: One of the elements that is controversial is the practice of generating use(r) data, or data-sets in general. This is particularly (and obviously) a bad practice in academia where faking data is a career ending sin. We want to urge you to steer clear from this. However, there are situations where it might be defendable to use genAI to extrapolate data-sets. The standard policy is that we do not allow this, if you feel that you need an exception you need to come talk to us. Only with explicit written permission from the course lecturers can you do this, and only within the boundaries that are then given. Additionally, you must provide a proper reference for the use of genAI, along with a clear explanation of how you used it and integrated into your process.
Alternative Assessment Instructions
You can submit a proposal for an alternative assessment via the Learning Platform, however, it is your responsibility to get in contact with the module coordinator to discuss your proposal well before the alternative proposal deadline.
Please note: This is to ensure there is enough time for you to register for standard assessment if your alternative assessment proposal is rejected or if it actually falls within the standard assessment guidelines.
In which cases should you consider an alternative assessment? There can be multiple scenarios that may require an alternative assessment for example:
Perhaps you would feel more comfortable showcasing your project work in a different format than that specified in the standard assessment instructions. Perhaps the project you have been working on throughout the semester would benefit from you showcasing your work in a different format (e.g., developing a short video to show your project work). What could an alternative assessment look like?
eg: You could propose being assessed via a 15-minute oral assessment. You could split the 15 minutes into 10 minutes to showcase your work in a structured manner, followed by 5 minutes of questions by the assessor/module coordinator.
