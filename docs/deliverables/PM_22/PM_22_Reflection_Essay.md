## How the frameworks changed what I thought I already knew

I came into this module thinking I had a head start. I had been building Mango Lab and Still for several months. I had already run a €400 Meta Ads campaign, conducted a real interview, built a business model canvas, and written a validation plan. Compared to someone just starting product development for the first time, I felt like I was close to the end of the L1 checklist before the semester even started.

That was not entirely wrong. But what the module taught me, framework by framework, is that there is a real difference between doing something and doing it with a structure that lets you learn from it. I had been moving fast. The course materials slowed me down in exactly the right places.

## The Mom Test and the question I should not have asked

The first framework that actually changed something for me was Rob Fitzpatrick's Mom Test, which Swantje introduced early in the module. The core rule is straightforward: never ask anyone whether your product is a good idea, because people lie to protect your feelings. Ask about their actual behaviour instead. Ask about the last time they did the thing you are trying to solve. Ask what they have already tried. Ask what the consequences were.

I had read the Mom Test before. I thought I understood it.

Then I ran the real interview with Fatema, the marketing coordinator at CODE University, and hit a wall about fifteen minutes in. I had been asking her about the content creation workflow, about what her week looked like, and she was giving me genuinely useful answers. Then I asked something about ad creation, trying to get to the core of what Still is solving, and she said, very politely: "that is not me, you would have to talk to the others."

She was redirecting me because ad creation genuinely is not her job. Two other people on the team handle that. I had asked a question that made sense from where I was sitting, trying to understand our potential users, and it turned out to not be about her at all.

Sitting with that after the interview, I realised I had been asking the wrong question. Not because I had violated the Mom Test by pitching, but because I had assumed the pain I was solving existed in a particular place in her working life, and it did not. She had real pain, specific and clear: posting Instagram stories every day with no planning horizon longer than tomorrow, waiting on one colleague (Marcel) to edit a 40-second video that then sat in a queue for a week until the moment had passed. That pain is real and significant. It is just not the one I was building for.

The Mom Test is often taught as a rule about not asking leading questions. What I actually got from applying it is something different: it forces you to follow the customer instead of leading them towards your hypothesis. I had been leading, gently, and Fatema was too polite to go along with it. She just redirected me back to her actual work.

That was the first moment where the module material changed a specific decision. I went back and reframed the appendix section to make clear that Fatema's primary pain was organic content and coordination, not ad creation, and that her value as an interview subject was as a signal about small-team constraints, not as a direct user of Still. That is a more honest reading of the data, and I would not have read it that way without thinking through what the Mom Test was actually telling me.

## Personas as research instruments

I had built personas for PM_16. They were fine. They looked like personas: a photo, a job title, a couple of quotes, some demographic information. They served their purpose as a communication artefact.

What this module asked me to do was treat each persona as a testable claim about a real person in the market. Every number in a persona is an assertion. When I wrote Marc Hoffmann, the B2B SaaS founder from Munich, into my first draft, I had him at €15,000 MRR after 18 months bootstrapped. I wrote that number from memory, from a rough sense of what an early-stage SaaS founder might look like.

Then I ran the research pass. The SaaSRanger 2024 dataset, aggregating about a thousand bootstrapped founders from MicroConf, Indie Hackers, and Freemius, says the median bootstrapped SaaS hits $1,000 MRR at 12 to 18 months and $5,000 MRR takes two to four years. €15,000 at 18 months would put Marc in the top five percent of bootstrapped SaaS founders. I had placed him there without thinking about it, because it felt right.

I dropped Marc to €8,000 MRR. He is still above the median, which is fine, because he should be slightly aspirational as a persona. But €8,000 is plausible. €15,000 was not.

I did that kind of correction eleven times across the six personas. Some numbers survived the research pass unchanged. Others did not. Sven's agency retainer went from a range I had estimated to a range I could cite from the Foxwell Digital benchmark, adjusted downward by 15 percent for DACH. His annual client churn framing got rewritten completely because I had been working from a "6 to 9 months" number that contradicts what the Focus Digital churn report actually says (32 percent annual churn, equivalent to an average tenure of about three years).

What the module taught me here is that personas built from memory are hypotheses. You do not actually know the numbers. And if you do not know the numbers, you do not know whether your ICP is a group that actually exists in the size you are assuming. That shift, from persona as communication to persona as research claim, is something I had not made on my own before this module.

## What the Hypothesis Board gave me retroactively

The framework I found most useful in the module was the Hypothesis Board, not because it changed what I was going to do next, but because it gave me a language for what I had already done.

Engine_001 was a €400 Meta Ads campaign I ran on a real client account. We spent €396.42, generated 12,847 impressions, 182 link clicks at a 1.42 percent CTR, and 79 qualified leads at a €5.02 blended cost per lead. Those numbers came out of the Meta dashboard and they told us a lot. The five-variant week cost €4.87 CPL where the single-variant week had cost €8.69. Brand-trained creatives hit 1.7 percent CTR; generic templates hit 0.71. LinkedIn died on day four at €127 CPL versus Meta's €4.87 on identical budget.

Before this module I would have described all of that as "running a campaign and reading the numbers." The Hypothesis Board forced me to re-read it differently. Every decision we made during that sprint was, in retrospect, a hypothesis we had held before we ran it and then checked after. H-02 (more creative variants reduce CPL) had a kill criterion: if the five-variant week does not beat the one-variant week on CPL, the iteration-speed thesis is wrong. It did beat it, by 44 percent. H-05 (channel mix improves outcomes) had a kill criterion: kill LinkedIn if CPL exceeds 3x Meta by day four. It hit 26x. We killed it. Saved roughly €140.

The point is that we had been doing hypothesis-driven testing without the vocabulary for it. Having the vocabulary matters more than I expected. When I went back to build the experiment-board slide for the deck, I could reconstruct the pre-committed kill criteria accurately, because they had actually been pre-committed, we just had not written them on a card. If I had built the Hypothesis Board first, I think we would have been more deliberate about defining the criteria before each test started, rather than relying on instinct in the moment. The instincts were good. The framework would have made them more defensible.

## The beachhead question as a forcing function

The beachhead market framework did something specific that I had not expected: it caught an inconsistency in my own materials.

When I handed my PM_16 artefacts to Claude for a synthesis pass early in this module, the model came back and told me, confidently, that my beachhead was iGaming. It cited specifics from my own documents. And I had to stop and say, no, that is wrong. The beachhead is SMBs. Always has been.

The confusion is fair. Somewhere in my PM_16 work I had explored iGaming as a potential vertical and the model had read that as a conclusion rather than as an exploration. But sitting with the correction, I realised that I had not been precise about the beachhead in my own materials, and that imprecision had been fine because I had always known what I meant. The framework forced me to write it down in a form that would not be misread.

The clear version is this: the beachhead is DACH SMBs who run their own paid social ads with a limited budget, limited time, and almost no visibility into whether the money they are spending is working. The two real clients to date, meetDWIGHT (a B2B SaaS product) and the marketing agency we did work for, both fit that shape. The five synthetic personas, Lisa, Marc, Sven, Anja, and Tobias, all fit different variants of that shape. iGaming was never the beachhead; it was an early hypothesis that did not survive contact with the actual ICP.

Writing a clear beachhead statement for the deck clarified something in my own head that had been slightly fuzzy. That is the thing I keep noticing about these frameworks: they are not just communication tools for presenting to assessors. They force a kind of precision that is hard to maintain when you are moving fast and everything makes intuitive sense in your head.

## The Lean Canvas and one structural difference that mattered

I had already built a Business Model Canvas for PM_16. The Lean Canvas is different in one specific way that I only really noticed when I built both and put them side by side.

The BMC starts at the top with Value Propositions and Customer Segments. The Lean Canvas starts at the top left with Problem. That is not a stylistic difference; it is an ordering choice that changes what you prioritise in a discovery phase. If you start with the customer segment and the value proposition, you can fool yourself into thinking you have already found product-market fit just by filling in the boxes. The problem box is harder to fill in honestly. It forces you to write down, clearly, what is actually failing for this person right now, before you say anything about what you are offering.

For Still specifically, the problem box is: SMBs running paid social ads have no fast way to test whether a different creative approach would outperform their current one, and no visibility into which of their targeting assumptions are actually correct. That is the thing Mango Lab discovered during the Engine_001 sprint. The knowledge gap is the product. Not the ad creation itself.

Writing that into the Lean Canvas problem box and then writing the value proposition box directly after it made the connection cleaner than anything I had built in PM_16. The BMC felt like a description of the business. The Lean Canvas felt like an argument.

## What I would do differently

Two things.

First, I would do at least two more real interviews before relying on the synthetic pool. The synthetic personas were honest research instruments for pressure-testing internal consistency. They are not a substitute for a real Marc Hoffmann telling me whether the creative-fatigue thesis actually matches his experience running LinkedIn ads for a bootstrapped SaaS. One real conversation would give me something none of the eleven citations in the persona brief can give me: a specific, unrehearsed reaction to a specific problem description.

Second, I would build the Hypothesis Board before the campaign, not after it. Engine_001 had pre-committed kill criteria in practice but not on paper. Having them on paper would have made the exit decisions easier to justify to a co-founder or a client who was watching the numbers go the wrong way. The discipline is mostly the same. The paper trail is not.

## What the module actually changed

I came in with a head start and I think I left with a different kind of advantage. Not just more artefacts, but a clearer sense of when to trust my own working-from-memory assumptions and when to check them. The frameworks in this module, the Mom Test, the persona-as-research-claim approach, the Hypothesis Board, the beachhead forcing function, the Lean Canvas ordering, all of them do the same basic thing: they make implicit assumptions explicit so you can test them.

That sounds obvious when you write it out. It is genuinely not obvious when you are moving fast and everything feels like it is working. The moments where the material slowed me down and made me check something I thought I already knew are the moments I am most grateful for. Most of the checks confirmed what I already believed. A few of them did not. The ones that did not were the ones worth the whole module.
